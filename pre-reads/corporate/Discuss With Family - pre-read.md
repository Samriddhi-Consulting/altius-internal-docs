# Discuss With Family

Rohit Menon is back, and he has brought the spreadsheet again. You have come to recognize the particular blue folder it lives in from across the gallery floor.

You are a Sales Executive at the project's sales gallery in Sector 150, Noida, two and a half years into the job, which is long enough to know that buyers come in three kinds: the tourists, the negotiators, and the ones who are already living in the flat in their heads and are terrified of the paperwork between them and it. Rohit is the third kind. This is his third visit in five weeks. He knows the maintenance rate per square foot. He has asked about the clubhouse gym equipment brand. On his last visit he stood on the 11th-floor terrace of the sample tower for six full minutes, looking east over the central green, saying nothing.

Most third visits end one of two ways, and you have stopped being surprised by either. Some buyers come back a third time to negotiate, having decided somewhere in visit two that the number is the only remaining problem. Rohit has never once asked for a discount. That, more than anything else on his file, is what makes this a different kind of stall.

It is the 26th. Your month closes in four days, one booking short of target, and the quarter's incentive slab is riding on it. You know better than to let a customer smell that. You have watched what happens when they do.

---

**The Flat**

The unit he wants is specific, which is always the tell. Tower B, 11th floor, the 2BHK plus study, 1,180 square feet, east-facing over the green. At the current Rs 10,100 per square foot, with EDC, IDC, one covered parking and GST, it lands at about Rs 1.19 crore all-in.

He has seen the cheaper version. On his second visit you walked him and his wife Divya through the west-facing equivalent, Rs 400 a square foot lighter, about Rs 4.7 lakh saved. Divya stood in the second bedroom, listened for a moment to the expressway, and said something quietly to Rohit in Malayalam. They came back to the east-facing floor plan and never mentioned the west stack again. You did not push either way that day, and you have wondered since whether that was instinct or just luck.

The inventory screen behind your desk, the live one, tells the rest: in that east-facing stack, two units remain. The 11th floor and the 4th. When this project launched, that stack had fourteen. You did not manufacture that number. You have simply watched it fall, three floors at a time, over five weeks, and Rohit has watched it too, on this same screen, without ever once asking you to hold one.

The project is RERA-registered, Towers A through D, possession in thirty months. You say that the way you say your own address now. It is true, and it is also not what is stopping him.

---

**The Money, As Far As He Has Said**

Rohit is a program manager at an IT services company, 34, methodical to a fault, the kind of buyer who reads a builder-buyer agreement clause by clause and asks what force majeure covers before he asks about the swimming pool. On visit two he produced, with visible pride, a home loan sanction letter: Rs 85 lakh pre-approved. The remainder, he said, was savings, and then, quicker, almost swallowed, "and some support from my father." He did not elaborate, and Divya glanced at him when he said it.

Two other moments from that visit have stayed with you, the way odd questions do. He asked, twice, in slightly different words: "If someone books, when exactly is the first ten percent due, I mean from which date does the clock start?" And later, at the payment-schedule board, he photographed the slab structure, 10 percent within 10 days of booking, 30 percent within 45 days, construction-linked after, and stood reading his own photo of a board that was right in front of him. You almost pointed that out. Something told you not to.

You have been doing this long enough to know that a man who asks when the clock starts is really asking about something the clock collides with, not the clock itself.

On the drive in this morning you replayed visit one in your head, mostly because Rohit's file is thin on paper and thick in memory. First visit: floor plans, sample flat, the usual tower walk. He took notes in the blue folder, not on his phone, which you have learned to read as a good sign. Second visit: Divya came, the west stack, the sanction letter, the two clock questions, the photograph of the payment board. He left without asking you to send anything by email, which is unusual for a man that prepared. Third visit: today. Spreadsheet buyers who return without asking for a concession are either about to book or about to vanish for six months. Your team lead's morning line landed because she has seen both endings often enough to know the difference is rarely the price.

There is a version of this you have seen before, in a different family, where the father's money arrives at the closing table as a surprise to everyone including the son, and the surprise curdles the whole transaction, questions about the unit that are really questions about being consulted too late. Rohit has not let that happen yet. Whatever he is protecting his father from, right now it looks a great deal like protecting his father from a number he has not finished deciding how to present.

---

**The Machinery, and the Two Ways to Use It**

The tools on your desk this week are, for once, all real.

The price revision is genuine: Rs 300 per square foot effective the 1st, circulated to channel partners in writing, you have the circular. On a 1,180-square-foot unit, that rate turns into real money fast, arithmetic you can walk him through rather than simply assert. The booking mechanics are standard and, used honestly, are actually the buyer's friend: Rs 1 lakh, cheque or NEFT, holds the unit and locks the price; it adjusts fully against the 10 percent; and per the builder's written policy it is fully refundable within seven days, Rs 10,000 processing deduction after that. Which means, though almost no one on this floor ever says it out loud, that a booking is less a leap than a seven-day option on a flat and a price, cheaper than the price revision it protects against.

You have also seen the machinery used the other way, and where that goes. Last year a colleague two desks down invented a competing buyer, "sir, another party is closing this exact unit tomorrow," and the customer, a quiet man, came back unannounced that evening and read the inventory screen for himself. It still showed three units available. He never said a word about it to anyone on the floor. He simply never came back, and the deal died in that one unremarked visit, along with the four referrals that customer's housing society would have produced. And you carry your own version: your second year, a genuine buyer, ready in every way except today, whom you pushed for a same-day cheque because your team lead was standing behind you. He said he understood. He never answered the phone again. You sold his flat to someone else eventually. It took five months.

The gallery head's line, the one thing he says that is worth keeping: "Pressure closes tourists. It loses buyers. A real buyer only needs two things, a reason it's safe to act, and a reason it's costly to wait. Both have to be true."

You think about that line when you look at the token terms handout on your desk. Refundable within seven days. Fully adjustable. Rs 10,000 processing if he changes his mind after the window. Most executives on this floor treat the token as a closing gimmick. You have started treating it as the honest answer to the question Rohit has not asked out loud yet: what if I am wrong to wait, and what if I am wrong to move?

Four days to month-end. One booking short. The circular is dated. The screen is live. Those are the only urgency tools you are allowed to use.

---

**What You Carry In**

For today: the live inventory screen, angled where he can see it himself; the circular about the price revision; the token terms, printed, refund window highlighted, because in your experience the highlighting does more than the discount you do not have authority to give; a costing sheet for the 11th-floor unit, all-in, no asterisks, built to be shown to a father who has never set foot in this gallery; and the weekend calendar, because Divya has been here twice but the family he keeps mentioning has a third member you have never met, and the flat is thirty months from possession but the decision, you suspect, is one conversation away, and it is not a conversation happening in this gallery.

You ran one piece of arithmetic last night that you will not say unless he earns it. If he books on the 30th, the 10 percent clock starts then, which puts the first slab payment after the 12th. You do not know yet whether his anxiety about the clock is about that date. You know only that he asked twice, and that a buyer who photographs the payment board is carrying a collision he has not named.

The costing sheet in your drawer is not the standard one. You rebuilt it last night with every line visible: base price, floor rise, EDC, IDC, parking, GST, maintenance corpus if applicable, total all-in. No asterisks, no "plus taxes as applicable" buried in the footer. If the family conversation he keeps mentioning includes a retired father who has never walked this gallery, the document that goes home tonight cannot look like something smuggled past him later.

Your team lead stopped by your desk before Rohit's appointment, not to pressure you exactly, though it landed that way regardless: "Third visit, no discount asked, spreadsheet buyer. These are the ones we lose to silence, not to the competition, six months of let me think about it and then nothing." She did not tell you what to do about it. She has closed enough of these herself to know that telling you would be the wrong kind of help.

The weekend calendar on your screen has two open slots for site visits with family. You have not mentioned them yet. Divya has stood on the terrace. The father has not. That asymmetry feels like the real shape of the stall, even before he says the word family out loud again.

---

**5:40 PM**

He goes through the costing sheet line by line, nodding at each number he already knows. He asks about the clubhouse completion date, which he also already knows. Outside the glass wall of the gallery, a family two appointments behind him is looking at the scale model of the towers, the father pointing something out to a child who has stopped listening. He looks, once, past your shoulder at the inventory screen, at the two green cells left in the east stack, and you watch him do the arithmetic he has been doing for five weeks.

Then he squares the sheet against the edge of the desk, the way careful men return borrowed things, and smiles the apology before he says it, gently, almost the way a man declines a second helping he genuinely wanted.

"It looks good. Honestly, it looks exactly right. But I need to discuss with my family first."
