# The Inconvenient Finding

The message from Rohan lands at 6:40 AM: "Stuck in Singapore, client crisis, flight tonight. You take the Malhotra readout. You built the analysis, you know it better than me anyway. Call me after."

Rohan is the partner on the engagement, and the only other person at the firm who has read every page of this deck as closely as you have. The Malhotra readout is at 11.

You are a Senior Consultant at the firm, three years in, and for the last nine weeks you have led the diagnostic on the retention engagement at Techvista Solutions: a 900-person IT services company losing people faster than it can hire them. You have run the numbers, coded the exit interviews, made the alumni calls, built the deck. You have presented plenty of findings before, always with a partner in the room to catch what falls.

You have never once delivered a finding this unwelcome without someone senior beside you to absorb the first reaction. You are about to find out how much of what a partner does in that chair is actually about the content, and how much is simply about being someone the client has already decided to trust.

At 11 AM it will be you, a deck, and Arvind Malhotra.

---

**The Man Who Built It**

Arvind Malhotra started Techvista in 2003 with eleven people in a basement in Nehru Place, and he can still name all eleven. Three of them are still with him. The company crossed 900 people last year, runs delivery for banking and insurance clients across India and the Gulf, and has never missed payroll, a fact he mentions in most town halls.

He is a founder of a particular kind: decisive, numbers-proud, loyal to the people who bled with him early, and famously generous when he believes in you. He is also a man who has done every job in the building at some point and therefore believes, not without reason, that he understands the building. He has told you, more than once, that he still reads every exit interview form himself before HR files it, which is either remarkable diligence or the reason he has stopped seeing what the forms do not say.

On attrition, his understanding is settled. "People leave for money," he told you both at the kickoff, leaning back. "Twenty years I have watched it. A boy gets thirty percent from a competitor, he goes. Loyalty has a price band." Attrition hit 24 percent last year against an industry average of 17, and two client escalations followed key exits, one of them a banking client that took its complaint all the way to the board. His fix is already in motion: a compensation correction, roughly Rs 9 crore annually, which he has told his board will bring attrition under 18 percent within a year. The board presentation was three weeks ago. Your engagement was commissioned, in his words, "to tell me where to spend it."

---

**What the Data Said**

The engagement did not go where he pointed it.

You coded 214 exits over eighteen months, triangulating exit interview forms, HR records, and 40 structured alumni calls. The exit forms, read naively, agree with Arvind: six in ten departing employees ticked "better offer elsewhere." That is the number his HR head has been showing him for two years.

The alumni calls broke it open. Person after person said a version of the same thing: the offer was the occasion, not the reason. They had started looking first. One of them, a delivery lead who left eight months ago, put it more plainly than most: "Nobody made me a better offer. I made myself available to hear one." You wrote that line down verbatim, suspecting you would need it more than any chart in the deck. Another, a tester who had been on the same account for four years, described weekly status calls where feedback arrived as a verdict, never a conversation. A third said she had stopped asking about promotion because the answer had stopped changing. When you coded underlying drivers, the ranking came out the way the slide shows. Pay is real, and pay is fourth.

Then the two findings that keep the deck honest. The firm's benchmarking shows Techvista already pays at market median, P50 to P55 across bands, a fact that surprised even you the first time you saw it, given how often Arvind describes his own pay philosophy as generous. A correction moves it to P60 and moves almost nothing else. And the concentration: 58 percent of regretted high-performer exits came from teams under just 4 of the company's 23 delivery managers. You checked the obvious rebuttal yourself, twice, because you knew you would need it: within those four teams, people paid above market median left at the same rate as people paid below it. Money did not protect a single one of them.

You built the deck so the ranking could be verified, not admired. Every percentage on the driver slide traces back to coded interview text. The concentration slide links to team IDs, not names. The benchmarking appendix shows band-by-band placement against market. Rohan reviewed it twice in Singapore and sent one note back: "If he says are you sure, do not answer with adjectives. Walk him through the within-band check. That is the whole meeting."

Last night, preparing, you looked up the four managers. All four joined before 2010. Two are from the original eleven. Arvind promoted each of them personally, and one of them gave the toast at his daughter's wedding. You sat with that fact longer than you sat with any chart, because a finding that names a system is safe to deliver, and a finding that quietly implicates four men Arvind Malhotra chose with his own hands is a different kind of morning. None of this is in the deck. All of it is in the room.

---

**Numbers You May Need**

Two hundred fourteen exits coded across eighteen months. Six in ten exit forms ticked "better offer." Coded drivers ranked manager-related at 31 percent, career growth 27, role clarity 19, compensation 14, other 9. Fifty-eight percent of regretted exits came from teams under four of twenty-three delivery managers. Pay sits at P50 to P55; the proposed correction aims at P60, about Rs 9 crore a year. Last year's attrition was 24 percent against an industry figure near 17.

The within-band comparison matters more than the percentages on the slide: above-median-paid people under those four managers left at the same rate as below-median. That is the answer to "are you sure" if he asks it the way founders ask things they already believe they know.

---

**What a Finding Costs**

There is a story they tell at the firm, usually late on engagement nights. A few years ago, a team ran a diagnostic for a retail client whose CEO was certain the problem was store-level discipline. The data said supply chain. The engagement manager, three weeks from renewal, reframed the findings until they "aligned with the client's operating hypothesis," a phrase people at the firm still repeat with a particular flatness in their voice. The client spent a year fixing discipline. The supply chain problem grew, the numbers got worse, and the client concluded, reasonably, that the firm's work was what had failed. No one from the firm has been back since. The engagement manager tells the story himself now, to juniors, as a service, and always ends it the same way: the renewal he saved lasted eleven months.

The other version of the failure has no story attached because it is quicker: consultants who arrive with a deck and a verdict, read the client his errors, and are politely never invited back. Nobody tells that story at team dinners, because there is no arc to it, just a client who stopped returning calls and a case study that quietly disappeared from the pitch deck. Rohan's phrase for the balance, said often: "We are paid to be right in a way the client can use."

Phase 2 of the Techvista engagement, designing the actual retention interventions, is scoped, priced, and sitting on Arvind's desk. Whether it gets signed is, everyone understands, a function of how this morning goes.

---

**Rohan's Text, Reread Twice**

Rohan sent one more message at 9:15, between his own meetings: "Don't soften it and don't spike it either. He's smarter than the room usually gives him credit for, and he'll smell a hedge before he smells a mistake. If he pushes, that's him doing his job. Your job is to still be standing there with the same ranking when he's done." You have read it more times than the message probably deserved, mostly because you do not yet know what "still standing there" looks like in practice, only that you will find out in under two hours.

You rehearsed the opening in the taxi. Not the slides. The first answer to pushback. Trigger versus cause as interpretation, not verdict. Nine crore as necessary but insufficient, so his board story can evolve instead of reverse. A next step he can inspect: methodology walk-through with HR, or a working session on manager practices under the four teams, without turning the readout into a loyalty test he cannot survive in his own building.

You also know what you must not do in that chair. Name the four managers as villains. Make the finding about his judgment. Trade the ranking for Phase 2. Recite the deck louder when he pushes. Hide behind "the data says" when he asks how the data was built. Soften pay to co-equal because the room got uncomfortable.

What you must do is narrower and harder: answer "are you sure" with evidence he can verify, honour twenty years without surrendering to it, separate trigger from cause, give him a path where nine crore is necessary but insufficient, and close on something inspectable that keeps the firm in the room.

---

**11 AM**

The corner office looks out over the Nehru Place skyline, and Arvind Malhotra has the deck open on the table in front of him. Through the glass wall behind him you can see the floor he built, rows of desks that used to fit in a basement, and somewhere out there, invisible from here, four managers who have no idea their teams are the reason this meeting is happening at all. He has already read it; he reads everything before meetings, another founding habit. He is on the slide with the driver ranking, and he has been on it, you suspect, for a while.

He looks up. Not angry. Concentrated, the way he must have looked at eleven people in a basement.

The conversation is about to start.
