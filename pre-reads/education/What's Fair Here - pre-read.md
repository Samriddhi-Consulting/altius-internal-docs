# What's Fair Here

The folder was heavier than it needed to be.

Vikas Anand had printed the same numbers twice. Once on his home printer that jammed on the third page. Once at the office after hours when the floor was empty and the AC had already been switched off. He stapled both sets, then removed the staples, then stacked the pages so the edges lined up. He put the stack in a manila folder that still smelled faintly of the stationery shop near his bus stop.

He told himself he was being thorough.

On the metro he opened the folder once, closed it, and watched his own reflection in the dark glass. A man with a folder looked like a man with a plan. A man without a folder looked like someone who might start talking about the wrong things.

His phone buzzed. A school group message about Meera's annual day rehearsal. He muted it without opening the thread. He would read it later, he told himself, when he knew what later looked like.

---

**The house that still has her drawings on the fridge**

The marital home sits in a quiet lane in Pune, joint names on the papers, no loan left to argue with. Roughly ₹1.4 crore if you believe the last broker who walked through and spoke too loudly about "good light." Joint savings and investments sit at about ₹22 lakh. Vikas put in more of that pile than his wife did, something like sixty to forty, if anyone is still counting contribution as if contribution were the whole story. Both names are on every account.

His take-home is ₹1,80,000 a month. Enough to feel solid in ordinary years. Not enough to feel careless when someone puts a long list of monthly obligations next to a child's age and asks you to multiply.

His wife has been clear, at least on paper and in the messages that arrive after Meera is asleep. She wants sole custody. She wants the house. She wants ₹35,000 a month until Meera turns eighteen: school fees, activities, general upkeep. She wants ₹8 lakh once, drawn from the joint savings, to settle what she calls the immediate gap.

Vikas has read those demands so many times that the words have stopped sounding like a marriage ending and started sounding like a spreadsheet he is supposed to solve.

He is forty-four. He works at Northline Consulting in a role that rewards people who stay calm in meetings. He has stayed calm. He has stayed reasonable. Reasonable is the word he keeps using with himself, as if saying it often enough might keep the ground from moving.

---

**What the file on your desk says**

You are a clinic student on placement at Rao Menon & Associates, family and matrimonial desk. Today you take a first consult.

Your supervisor, Advocate Priya Rao, left a sticky on the intake sheet before she stepped into a mediation that overran:

> Vikas Anand. 11:15. First meeting. He asked for "someone who can walk the numbers." Do not invent comfort. Do not promise outcomes. Listen before you calculate. Debrief with me after.

The intake sheet is thin. Self-referred after a friend mentioned the firm's family desk. No prior counsel on file. Status: married, separation in progress, one child.

Client snapshot (lookup facts): **Vikas Anand, 44**, mid-level at Northline Consulting. Monthly take-home **₹1,80,000**. Marital home joint, about **₹1.4 crore**, no outstanding loan. Joint savings and investments about **₹22 lakh** (he estimates roughly 60/40 contribution in his favour). Child: **Meera, age 9**. Stated demands in his summary: sole custody; home in full; **₹35,000/month** until Meera turns 18; **₹8 lakh** one-time from joint savings.

Priya's second line on the sticky, smaller:

*First consults that arrive as maths problems are still first consults.*

*Fairness is often the wrong first question. Listen before you calculate.*

You do not know his wife. You do not know what has been said in rooms you were not in. You know what is on the sheet, and what a man looks like when he has decided to be reasonable in public.

---

**The man who arrives with the folder closed**

At 11:12 the receptionist buzzes Meeting Room 2.

Vikas Anand is dressed like someone who chose the jacket that would look least dramatic under office lights. He sits carefully. He places the folder on the table and does not open it yet. His hands are still.

He glances once at the glass wall, as if checking whether anyone is watching him practise being fine.

"You're the student," he says. Not unkind. Checking the room the way he checks a spreadsheet: what is here, what is missing.

"I'm covering the intake. Advocate Rao will debrief with me after. I've read the sheet."

He nods. The nod of a man who has already decided he will not waste time.

"Good," he says. "Then we should start with what she's asking for."

Outside, a printer coughs and stops. Somewhere down the corridor a mediation door opens and closes. The air conditioning hums like a thing that has been humming for years.

Vikas rests two fingers on the edge of the folder. He does not open it. Not yet.

He looks at you the way people look when they have rehearsed the first sentence and need the second person in the room to let them say it.

He is ready to begin. The folder is still closed. The numbers are still real. What is not on the sheet is the fear that sits under "what's fair": the fear of becoming a father Meera has to schedule. That fear will not open itself. Spreadsheet competence can close the door on it for the whole meeting if you answer fairness as a closed calculation too early.

Priya's mediation still has not released her. The corridor clock says you have the room. Vikas has the folder. Between those two facts is the only consult that matters today.

---

**What "reasonable" costs**

Reasonable is how Vikas survived the last months at Northline. Reasonable is how he answered his wife's late-night messages without raising his voice. Reasonable is how he explained to his sister that he would not "fight dirty," as if dirt were the only alternative to a spreadsheet. Reasonable is also how a man can arrive at a law clinic asking for someone who can walk the numbers, because numbers feel safer than saying he is afraid of becoming a calendar parent.

Meera is nine. Annual day rehearsals continue whether or not the adults in Meeting Room 2 finish their fairness maths. The school group chat will keep buzzing. The fridge in the Pune house still has her drawings. None of that is on the intake sheet. All of it is why the sticky note told you to listen before you calculate.

You have seen clinic students before who treat first consults as valuation exercises. They leave with clean notes and a client who never said the sentence that mattered. You also know the other failure: catching the house concession and missing the second half of the sentence about scheduling a daughter. Competence that misses the person is still a miss.

The air conditioning hums. The mediation door opens and closes again. Vikas's two fingers stay on the folder edge. He is waiting for you to decide whether this meeting is about what she is asking for, or about what he is afraid of becoming while he pretends the only question is fairness.

**What you cannot promise**

You cannot promise custody outcomes. You cannot invent comfort. You cannot trade legal doctrine for trust. You can notice when a man who arrived to walk the numbers starts talking about a daughter who might have to schedule him. That noticing is the work. The folder can wait until the person is in the room.

Outside, Pune traffic continues. Inside, the consult is about to start.



---

**Before the buzz**

You read the sticky twice. Listen before you calculate. First consults that arrive as maths problems are still first consults. You have sat in enough clinic rooms to know how fast a folder can become the whole relationship. Vikas's jacket is the least dramatic one he owns. That is not nothing. Men who choose undramatic jackets are often already performing calm for an audience that includes themselves.

Meera's annual day rehearsal message is still muted on his phone. He will unmute it after this meeting, or he will not. What happens in the next twenty minutes will decide whether he leaves with a valuation plan or with a sentence about the kind of father he refuses to become. Priya will debrief you either way. Only one of those debriefs will include the word schedule spoken by the client, not invented by you.

The glass wall shows a slice of corridor. A clerk walks past with a stack of cause lists. Somewhere a kettle clicks off. Vikas has not opened the folder. That restraint is the opening. Treat it as data.

Vikas came for numbers because numbers feel like control. Meera's drawings on the fridge are not a line item. The scheduled-parent fear is not a line item either. If this consult becomes only a valuation of house, savings, and monthly rupees, you will have been helpful and still missed him. Priya's sticky was not decoration. Listen before you calculate is the pedagogy and the craft. The folder can open after the person does. The printer coughs again. He still has not opened the folder. That is your cue to leave room instead of filling it with fairness maths he already rehearsed on the metro.

He muted the school thread on the metro because he could not hold Meera's rehearsal and the spreadsheet at once. That split attention is the case. Your job is not to pick the spreadsheet for him. It is to notice when fairness talk is covering a fear of becoming someone his daughter schedules. Leave the room for that sentence. Then the numbers can earn their place.

The corridor clock does not care about fairness. Meera's rehearsal does not wait for a perfect valuation.

Vikas booked a valuation consult and paid for it in advance. On paper this is a division-of-assets matter: the flat, the joint account, the monthly maintenance figure. The custody question is not formally on today's agenda, because Vikas has not put it there. The schedule he is quietly afraid of is the one where he sees his daughter on alternate weekends by an order he did not write. Priya will read your file note tonight either way. He arrived early, and he has not touched the water on the table. The folder sits closed on the desk between you, exactly where he set it down. Outside, Pune traffic keeps its own time, and the buzzer for the next consult has not sounded yet.
