# The Home Loan

Most walk-ins do not come back. Nikhil Bhatt booked a slot.

You are a Home Loan Advisor at the Noida Sector 18 branch of the bank, three years into retail assets, which is long enough to read a customer from how they enter before they have said a single word about what they actually want. Ten days ago Nikhil came in with a folder of printouts, asked four precise questions, wrote your answers down word for word, and left without applying. This morning he emailed the branch asking for thirty minutes with you, specifically you, at 11.

Attached to the email, without being asked, were his salary slips, Form 16, and the builder's cost sheet, labelled and dated. Also attached, in a separate PDF he had made himself, was a comparison table of two banks with several cells marked "need to confirm." You have had customers bring spreadsheets before. You have never had one bring a PDF with a version number in the filename.

The first row of the table was Rate of Interest. His other bank's column said 8.50. Yours said 8.65.

---

**The Flat and the Clock**

The flat is a Rs 78 lakh 2BHK in a RERA-registered project in Noida Extension, under construction, possession promised in two years. Nikhil and his wife Shruti booked it three weeks ago with Rs 5 lakh of their savings, most of what nine years of salary had accumulated after his sister's wedding and his father's angioplasty, two expenses he mentioned in the same breath as if they were the same kind of event, which in a way, in his family, they were. The loan he needs is Rs 62 lakh over 20 years.

The builder's first demand letter is already issued. Payment is due in 19 days. After that, the booking terms say, late interest starts, and the family group chat, which now includes a retired uncle who once worked in a cooperative bank and has opinions, reminds him of this daily.

One fact sits in your favour before you say a word, and you checked it this morning: the project is on the bank's approved list, legal and technical verification of the property already done at project level. For a clean salaried file, that is the difference between disbursing in twelve working days and discovering, in week three, that the other lender's empanelled lawyer has a query about the builder's title chain. It is a real advantage. It is also the kind of advantage that sounds like a sales line unless you explain how it actually works.

The clock is not abstract for Nikhil. Nineteen days is a number he has written at the top of his comparison PDF in red. You noticed it when you opened the attachment at your desk before the branch opened.

---

**The Man Across the Desk**

Nikhil is a senior QA engineer at an IT services firm, 31, the kind of customer who has watched eleven video explainers titled some version of "Home Loan MISTAKES to avoid" and emerged knowing many facts and trusting none of them, the occupational hazard of someone whose entire job is finding the bug nobody else caught. His questions last time were about processing fees, prepayment charges, and whether "floating" meant the bank could change the rate whenever it wanted. He asked that last one twice, slightly differently, the way people re-ask a question when the first answer was accurate but not reassuring.

Two things he said in passing stayed with you. First: "I'm the first one in my family doing this. My father never took a loan in his life. He thinks EMI is a trap." Second, quieter, while gathering his printouts: "My CIBIL is 764. There was one late payment on a credit card two years ago, 38 days, my fault, I was between projects. Every website says 750-plus is fine but every forum has someone rejected anyway." He laughed when he said it. It was not entirely a laugh. You noted, at the time, that he said "my fault" before you had asked whose fault it was.

Shruti, who teaches in a primary school, could not come today. Whatever he carries out of this meeting, you know, will be re-argued at their dining table tonight against the uncle's forwarded advice, in a conversation you will never see and can only try to prepare him to win on his own.

He chose you for the second meeting, not the branch queue. That is itself a signal. Last time you did not push an application form across the desk. You answered the four questions and let him leave with his notes. In retail assets, that is sometimes treated as a lost lead. You have never been sure it was.

---

**The Other Offer, and the Honest Math**

You have seen the competing offer; he attached that too. Meridian Housing Finance, 8.50 percent floating, processing fee waived under a festival scheme. You know Meridian's retail desk. The offer is genuine, the waiver is real, and their advisor, by the evidence of Nikhil's annotations, answered his emails in single lines.

You did the arithmetic last night because you knew this meeting would start there, and you have found, over three years, that a number you compute in front of a customer always lands weaker than one you already had ready. Fifteen basis points on Rs 62 lakh over twenty years: about Rs 580 a month. Around Rs 1.4 lakh over the full tenure, if either rate stood still for twenty years, which no floating rate ever has. It is real money. It is also less than what one month of a stalled disbursal past the demand-letter date would cost him, and nowhere near what a wrong insurance decision would.

Which brings you to the part of your own house you have to be honest about, the part no advisor enjoys getting to. Your processing fee is Rs 12,500 plus GST, and there are legal-technical charges of about Rs 6,000. His comparison table has a "need to confirm" cell waiting for exactly those numbers. And the branch's single-premium insurance bundle, about Rs 1.9 lakh, usually financed invisibly into the loan, is on your scorecard this month. Your branch manager asked about attach numbers at the morning huddle, by name, with a smile that was not one.

You also know, because you looked it up for a customer in March and never forgot it, that a standalone term plan covering the same liability costs a healthy 31-year-old roughly a third of the bundled premium. Term cover for the loan amount is genuinely sensible for a first-time borrower with a young family. The bundled product is one way to buy it. It is not the only way, and it is not free money.

The advisor who trained you, Mrs D'Souza, retired last year with a client list that still calls her for advice on loans she can no longer sell, which the branch finds mildly ridiculous and you have never once found ridiculous. She had one line about this job that the branch has half-forgotten and you have not: "A rate wins you a comparison. An explanation wins you a family. Only one of those refers you their brother-in-law."

You have also seen the other road. A colleague at your previous branch closed a first-timer at a great rate with the bundle folded in unmentioned. The customer discovered the Rs 1.8 lakh line item at disbursal, escalated to the nodal officer, cancelled, and posted the entire email thread in a home-buyers' forum where, as far as you know, it still ranks near the top of search results for the branch's name. BFSI ethics is not an abstract training slide in this room. It is a search result.

---

**The Morning Huddle**

Your branch manager's exact words this morning, to the whole floor, not just you: "Insurance attach is soft this quarter. I don't want to hear about rate pressure, I want to see the number move." He said your name once, lightly, the way managers say a name to make a general statement feel personal without quite being unfair about it.

You have not decided yet what you are going to do about that, walking in. You have decided what you cannot do. You cannot bury the premium. You cannot push the bundle as mandatory. You cannot let the scorecard turn this meeting into the forum story your colleague wrote.

Nikhil's CIBIL score is 764. The one late payment was 38 days, 26 months ago, fully cleared since. At 764 with stable salaried income his approval odds at either bank are strong. One more hard enquiry moves nothing meaningfully. You know that. He does not fully believe it yet. How you say it will matter as much as whether you say it.

Meridian's branch is closer to his office. That row is on his sheet too. You cannot move the branch. You can move the quality of what he carries out.

---

**What You Carry In**

What you can honestly offer today: the all-in comparison he has been trying to build alone, with your own bank's fees filled into his "need to confirm" cells first, not last. A document checklist written for his exact case: salaried, joint application with Shruti, under-construction flat, this specific builder. The real sequence from application to disbursal against his 19-day clock, with the approved-project advantage explained rather than asserted. A straight answer on 764 and the 38-day-late entry, and on what one more hard enquiry does and does not do. The insurance conversation done clean: term cover as a genuinely good idea, the bundle as one optional way to buy it, the cheaper way named out loud.

What you cannot offer: 8.50. The rate is the rate.

If the numbers still favour Meridian after honesty, you can still compete on service and certainty: who picks up when the lawyer has a query, who stays reachable when Shruti has a question at 9 PM, whether the file moves in twelve working days or sits in a queue while the demand letter clock runs. That competition only works if the math in the room was honest first.

In your drawer: a blank comparison printout with your bank's fee column ready to fill, a checklist template you adjusted last night for under-construction salaried files, and your direct line written on a card you have used twice before for customers who needed a name, not a toll-free tree.

---

**11 AM**

He arrives at 10:58 with the folder, thicker now, and his own printed comparison on top. He sits, accepts the water, and lines his pen up against the folder's edge before he speaks. When he does, it comes out slightly rehearsed, the way a careful man delivers a line he has practised on the metro. Somewhere behind you, a queue token machine calls the next number in the flat, uninterested voice these machines always use, and neither of you looks over.

"I've read everything. I've compared everything. Your bank is at 8.65, the processing fee is twelve and a half thousand, and honestly your branch is farther from my office too." He turns his comparison sheet around to face you, one finger on the first row, and asks the next question evenly, not quite as a challenge, more like a man reading off his own list because it has to be asked before anything else can be.

"The other bank is offering me less. Why shouldn't I just go there?"
