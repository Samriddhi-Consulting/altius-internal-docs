# The Green RAG

Omar Al-Rashidi does not tell this story often.

You heard it once, on a call that ran past midnight in March, three weeks before any of this became a problem. He had called to explain something about how the Al Noor relationship actually worked, something he wanted you to have before the quarter turned.

A container of industrial lubricants had been sitting at Jawaharlal Nehru Port for eleven days. Customs hold. A commodity code on the manifest had been flagged, and until someone physically walked the documentation through the correct office, forty drivers and their rigs were idle in a Bhiwandi warehouse. The goods had already crossed the Arabian Sea, cleared Jebel Ali, and unloaded at the terminal. The freight forwarder had tried to move it twice. Al Noor's India operations manager had tried once. Neither had made progress.

The problem was the kind that requires someone to be at the right desk with the right documentation and enough patience to stay until it moves.

Omar drove to Nhava Sheva himself.

He left before five in the morning. He brought the documentation, printed and tabbed and translated where the customs form required it, and spent four hours across a desk from a port official who had seen every version of this problem and was not inclined to hurry. Omar was patient. He did not invoke the bank. He answered each question and waited for the next.

By noon the matter was resolved. He stepped outside and called Faisal Al Noor in Dubai.

He spoke first in Arabic. You heard this part second-hand and you have thought about it since: that the call shifted mid-sentence from the measured English he used in meetings to something faster and more private. He was reassuring Faisal before he was explaining anything. The container would move Thursday. The drivers should be at the Bhiwandi gate by Saturday morning.

The container moved on Thursday.

Omar sent you a message Saturday evening. *Al Noor back on track.* You replied with a thumbs up. You were at dinner, and for a moment you felt the quiet satisfaction of having hired the right person for a difficult mandate.

That was March. It is now the last Thursday in June, and Omar is sitting across from you on the twenty-seventh floor in Nariman Point with two coffees on the desk between you and nothing yet to say.

---

**Al Noor**

Faisal Al Noor's grandfather arrived in Dubai from Oman in the mid-seventies with an engineering certificate and a systematic mind. The original business was a single cold-storage facility in Jebel Ali, built for Gulf exporters who needed reliable temperature-controlled handling and found it harder to source than anyone admitted. Faisal's father expanded into freight forwarding when the Gulf corridor opened in the late nineties. By the time Faisal took over in 2014, Al Noor had a specific reputation: the company you called when a shipment mattered and a delay was not an option.

Faisal was thirty-four when he took over. London MBA, the first in the family to have spent years working outside the Gulf before returning. He expanded the business into India that same year. The decision cost more than the board had sanctioned and took three years longer than anyone had forecast. In the second year of the expansion, the board meetings were not easy for Faisal.

He held it together.

By the time Meridian Gulf & India Bank became the account bank for the India operations, the subsidiary employed more than eight hundred people across three cities. Drivers and warehouse staff, freight coordinators, a small logistics technology team that Faisal had insisted on building in-house against every piece of advice he received. Santosh, the India operations manager, had been with the company since it opened, building the team from eleven people over nine years.

The LC and working capital lines were what kept the import cycle running. Without those lines, the warehouses stopped receiving goods. Santosh had told Omar once, and Omar had mentioned it to you in passing on a call some months ago, that the business could not sustain payroll past three weeks if the cycle went down.

You had filed that away.

---

**How He Was Hired**

Meridian Gulf & India Bank had held the Al Noor mandate through its Dubai desk for two years before Omar joined the India team. A working capital line, some LC business, a credit officer who had met Faisal at two industry events and filed relationship notes that were accurate and professionally thin. The mandate existed. What sat behind it was not much.

You met Omar at a trade finance conference in Dubai two and a half years ago. He was thirty-one, on the GCC corporate desk at a regional bank. Over coffee he walked you through something most Indian corporate banks misread consistently: the way decision-making worked in Gulf family businesses, the way trust functioned in those mandates, and why firms that led with their portfolio kept losing accounts to banks that had taken the time to understand the relationship differently. He was interested in the subject. It was apparent in how he spoke.

You came back to Mumbai and sent his name to HR.

He joined six weeks later. The brief was simple: build the GCC book on the India desk. Al Noor was the anchor.

In the first year he rebuilt the mandate into something real. He visited Nhava Sheva four times. He met Faisal in Dubai twice. He spent time with Santosh learning the India operation from the inside, which no one from the bank had done before. By the end of year one, the exposure had grown from two hundred and eighty crore to three hundred and sixty crore, and he had brought two new GCC-affiliated clients to the book.

By the end of year two, the Al Noor exposure stood at four hundred and twenty crore. The book covered working capital lines, trade finance, and the LC facilities the import cycle ran on. Faisal had come to call Omar first when something went wrong on the India side. Omar had built the relationship until it worked that way.

Your promotion case last fiscal cited the Al Noor expansion by name.

---

**The Green RAG**

The RAG call runs every last Thursday of the month. Forty minutes. Each RM walks through the monitored accounts and classifies each one. The classifications feed into Group Credit's portfolio view and, beyond that, into the regulatory data warehouse, where they constitute the desk's formal assessment of credit quality at that point in time.

You set up the ritual when you took the regional head role. You run it every month without fail, because you have seen what happens to desks that do not.

Al Noor had been on the monitoring list since February. The cash cycle had compressed over the quarter: Gulf demand softening, Nhava Sheva port volumes down, Indian buyer payment terms stretching while the supplier side held. Omar had flagged it himself in February, unprompted, before you had pulled the numbers.

In April, you ran the DSCR before the call. The number came back at 0.89. The covenant floor was 1.10.

You had that figure on your screen when Omar reached Al Noor on the agenda.

"The shipping delay from last month has pushed cash conversion a little," he said. Even, not hedging. "One shipment clears in the next fortnight and the ratio comes back above covenant. Seasonal. I'd keep it Green."

You looked at 0.89.

You had been running covenant calculations for eleven years. You knew what the number meant. You also knew about Nhava Sheva in March. You knew Omar had driven there at five in the morning rather than let a container sit. You had watched him manage this account for two years without leaving a problem unresolved that he could have resolved himself. You believed the shipment would clear.

"Okay," you said. "Green."

You moved to the next account.

The month closed. Group Credit's portfolio view logged Al Noor as Green. The data warehouse logged it Green. The covenant breach went into an automated monitoring queue.

---

**What You Read This Morning**

You called Omar at eight, before the day had started properly. You said you wanted to run through Al Noor before the afternoon.

He was measured. He said the situation was more complex than last month's note reflected, that he had been in conversations with Faisal about near-term options, and that he expected to have something concrete to bring to Credit by the end of the week. He asked you to hold off on any escalation until after you had spoken.

You had a meeting starting. You said you would talk this afternoon.

After the call, you opened the file.

The credit memo is dated five weeks ago. Omar authored it. It describes Al Noor's cash position as seasonal demand compression with a temporary logistics bottleneck at Nhava Sheva. Outlook stable. It does not mention the covenant breach. It does not flag for Credit review. Filed. No follow-up.

The DSCR you ran this morning: 0.89 against a covenant floor of 1.10. The breach has been in place for six weeks.

The LC facility renews Friday. Without it, the import cycle stops.

The account is Omar's, and has been since before you took this desk. Whatever the file says now, he built the relationship, and he signed the review that kept it classified Green. The exposure is not the kind the bank absorbs quietly. Credit's queue closes at 6 PM regardless of what passes between the two of you before then, and the written explanation that follows will carry a name on it. You have read the memo enough times this morning to know it will not read any better on the next pass.

You thought about April.

---

**Thursday, 2:15 PM**

The Group Credit email arrived at 11:04.

A single page. An automated covenant monitoring flag generated six weeks ago. The data warehouse had pulled Al Noor's financials, calculated the DSCR, and flagged the breach. When the analyst opened the queue this week, she found the CRM classification had remained Green throughout. The email asks for a written explanation. It notes that misclassification of a stressed account could constitute a conduct matter. Response required by 6 PM today.

You read the credit memo again after that. You read the covenant calculation. You went through the LC renewal paperwork.

At 2:05, Omar sent a message asking for fifteen minutes.

He is in the room now. Two coffees. He set one on the corner of your desk and took the chair across from you without waiting to be asked. Rain on the glass, flat and grey over the city. The email is open on your monitor. He can see the screen from where he's sitting but not the text.

He picks up his coffee.

He says nothing yet.
