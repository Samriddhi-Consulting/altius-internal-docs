# Disagree & Commit

The email went out at 9:00 AM Monday, company-wide, from the CEO's office. Subject: "AI-First Delivery Policy, effective immediately."

You are a Delivery Team Lead at Arka Digital Services, a 350-person firm that builds and runs software for banks, insurers, and retail brands. You have eight reports and thirteen months in the role, long enough to have opinions about your own management style and not quite long enough to fully trust them under pressure. You learned about the policy the way your team did, by reading it, except you got a 30-minute managers' call on Friday, which is what now passes for a head start.

The policy, stripped of its slide gloss: the company's approved AI coding and content assistant becomes mandatory on all billable delivery work. Prompts and outputs are logged on company systems. A new metric, AI-assisted share of output, enters appraisals for one year. Forty hours of paid training per person. The FAQ says, in bold, "This policy is about competitiveness, not headcount," the kind of line that reads reassuring right up until someone reads it twice.

Your team's group chat, by 9:20, had gone from links to memes to silence. Silence, you have learned in thirteen months, is the one to watch. Someone had posted a gif at 9:04. Nobody had posted anything at all since 9:12. You typed a reply twice and deleted it both times, because anything you said in that channel would be read as position before you had said it to the one person whose reaction you actually needed to see first.

---

**Why It's Rushed**

You know something most of your team doesn't, because it came up on the Friday call under a slide marked confidential.

In the last two quarters, two of the firm's five largest clients moved delivery work to competitors quoting around 30 percent lower, both of them pitching AI-assisted teams. Neither departure was framed as a complaint about quality; both procurement emails, which made their way around the account leads eventually, used almost identical language about "efficiency parity." A third client, an insurer whose renewal your own team supports, stayed only after leadership promised comparable efficiency by Q3 and priced the renewal as if it already existed. The policy isn't really a philosophy at all. It's a promise the company has already sold to someone else.

You believe that part. You have seen the rate cards, the same ones every account lead has seen in the last two quarterly reviews, and the direction of the market is not really in dispute inside the building, whatever people say in the group chat. If the market reprices what a delivery hour costs, the firm can either learn the new arithmetic or shrink inside the old one.

What you are less at peace with is the fine print. On the Friday call you asked, on record, in front of eleven other team leads, whether prompt logging was necessary and whether an adoption metric belonged in appraisals, where it reads less like enablement and more like a loyalty test. The delivery head's answer was courteous and final: the logging is a client-confidentiality requirement, and the metric stays for year one, "because what gets measured gets moved." You said okay, because the call was ending and because you are thirteen months into this job. It has sat with you since, in the specific way a question you already knew the answer to still manages to sting when it's confirmed out loud.

Your private position is split by design, and you will need to say all of it out loud if Sameer asks directly. You agree with the direction. You argued against the logging detail and the appraisal metric in the room where it counted, and you lost, for now. You are still the person who has to land this on your team starting with him, which means the answer cannot be "management decided" and it also cannot be "between us, I think it's nonsense." Both of those fail in rooms you have already watched fail this week.

---

**Sameer**

Sameer Kulkarni has been at the firm eight years, on your team for three, and he was writing production code when you were in college placement training. His reviews are the reason your team's defect rate is the region's lowest, a fact you have cited in your own appraisal more than once without ever quite crediting him by name in writing. Two juniors on the team, both quick adopters of every new tool, still take their hardest design questions to him, and he gives them real time, the way good seniors do.

At Monday's team meeting, where you presented the policy, Sameer said exactly one thing: "So the company will read our prompts and grade us on how much of our work we didn't do ourselves." He said it flatly, wrote something in his notebook, and did not speak again. Yesterday he booked thirty minutes with you. The invite has no title.

You have done your quiet homework since, mostly because thirty minutes with no title on the invite is not a meeting you walk into cold. His adoption of the pilot tools, during the optional phase, was the lowest on the team; the two juniors he mentors were the highest, by a wide margin, the kind of gap that shows up on a dashboard before anyone thinks to ask why. And you remember, because he mentioned it once and never again, that his wife spent nine years at an edtech company that restructured her role out last year. The town hall where they announced it used the phrase "AI-led efficiency." He took two days of leave and came back and said nothing.

Sameer is not a loud resistor. He does not perform outrage in meetings or rally the juniors against you. He writes careful code, runs careful reviews, and when he disagrees he does it the way he disagrees with a design doc: once, precisely, and then he waits to see whether anyone in the room is actually listening. That is harder to manage than anger, because anger gives you something to respond to and precision gives you a question you have to answer honestly.

---

**The Two Juniors**

Both of the juniors Sameer mentors have told you, separately and unprompted, some version of the same thing: they got good fast because he made them explain their reasoning out loud before he'd approve anything, tool-assisted or not. Neither of them thinks the AI assistant replaces that. One of them said so directly, in a one-on-one two weeks ago, without you asking: "The tool tells me what's possible. Sameer's the one who taught me what's actually good." You didn't write it down at the time. You wish you had, walking into this room.

---

**What You've Seen Go Wrong**

You have watched two versions of this conversation fail in other teams already, in one week.

One lead forwarded the policy with the line "orders from above, don't shoot the messenger," and his team took the hint: if he doesn't own it, we don't either. Their adoption numbers are already the region's lowest, three weeks in, and his boss has noticed whose team they are, in the specific way bosses notice things without yet saying anything about them directly. Another lead performed enthusiasm so bright it curdled; her senior-most engineer resigned Thursday, and the exit note, which got around the way exit notes always do, said he would rather leave than be "cheerled into irrelevance by someone who doesn't believe a word of it."

Both leads, you remember thinking, answered the same unasked question wrongly, in opposite directions, which is its own kind of lesson about how much room there actually is to get this wrong. The team is rarely really asking whether the policy is good. More often the real question, underneath, is whether they can trust what their lead tells them about it.

There is a third failure mode you have heard about but not seen in person on your floor: the manager who treats the policy debate as if it were still open, walks Sameer through every clause as though a better argument might reverse Monday's email, and ends by promising to "take it upstairs" in a way that sounds brave and delivers nothing. That conversation leaves the report feeling managed and lied to in the same breath, because the promise was never theirs to make.

---

**What You Carry In**

What is fixed: the policy is live, the logging is contractual, the metric stays for year one. What is genuinely open: the feedback window closes end of month, and the delivery head has said, credibly, that the metric's design weights and the logging scope will be reviewed against quarter one data; your written submission goes in Friday, and you have already drafted three paragraphs of it, mostly about people like Sameer. What is yours regardless: how work is distributed on your own team, how review and mentoring effort is recognised in your own appraisal inputs, and what you choose to say in the next thirty minutes.

HR's rollout guidance to managers had one line you underlined: "Do not promise exceptions. Do not manufacture agreement."

You have already drafted three paragraphs for Friday's feedback submission, mostly about people like Sameer: that the metric should weight review and mentoring contribution, that logging scope should be reviewed against actual client-confidentiality incidents in Q1, that adoption without judgment is not the same thing as quality. You believe those paragraphs are honest. You also know the delivery head said review happens against quarter one data, not against manager wish lists, and that "honest odds" is a phrase you will need to use in the room if you want Sameer to trust you after this meeting.

Your delivery head called you separately yesterday afternoon, after the Friday session, just for a minute: "I know Sameer's meeting is on your calendar. He's your best reviewer and I'd rather lose the metric argument for a year than lose him for good. Handle it like he matters, because he does." It was, in its way, the most useful thing anyone said to you all week, and also not something you can repeat to Sameer without it sounding like a favour he didn't ask for.

---

**4:30 PM**

Sameer comes in on time, closes the door, and sits with his notebook squared in front of him, which is how he arrives at code reviews where he intends to say something difficult. Through the glass you can see the rest of the team pretending not to watch the clock on this particular meeting more than any other this week.

"I've read the policy four times," he says. "I understand the market logic, I'm not a child. But logging every prompt I write, and grading me on how much of my work a tool did, that is not engineering, and you know my work better than anyone here." He turns the notebook ninety degrees, a small precise movement, and looks straight at you, the same steady look he gives a pull request he's about to reject for reasons he's already decided are non-negotiable.

"Before we talk about any of it, I want to know one thing. Do you even agree with this?"
