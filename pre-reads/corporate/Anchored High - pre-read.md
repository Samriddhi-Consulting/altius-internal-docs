# Anchored High

The renewal quote from Varghese Precision Moulds arrived eleven days ago, and it has been sitting in your inbox like a stone: 18 percent up on last year, a number you have now read often enough that it no longer even surprises you on the screen.

You are the Procurement Officer for direct materials at Helix MedDevices, which builds infusion pumps sold to hospitals in India and the Gulf. The housings, the precision moulded shells and internal brackets of the flagship pump, come from Varghese Precision Moulds in Kochi, and have for nine years. Rs 2.1 crore of annual buy. The new quote adds roughly Rs 38 lakh to that, and your Procurement Head's reaction, scrawled in the margin of the printout on your desk, is one line: "Settle at 6 max, or we finally test the market properly."

Tomorrow you fly to Kochi to sit with Joseph Varghese himself. It will be the first supplier-owner negotiation you run alone. Your Procurement Head offered, almost in passing, to sit in on the call. You said you'd handle it, and have spent eleven days since wondering whether that was confidence or just pride talking.

---

**Why This Supplier**

The obvious question, the one your Procurement Head half-asked with that margin note, is why fight for this vendor at all. You have two cheaper quotes in the file.

You have also read the file properly, which is the difference.

The first alternative, 22 percent below Joseph, is a volume moulder that has never held medical-grade tolerances; their one attempt at a regulated audit two years ago is quietly famous in the industry for how it went, the kind of story procurement people tell each other at conferences with a specific, knowing shake of the head. The second, 15 percent below, is the shop Helix uses for a smaller category of non-critical mouldings, and their pattern is on record in your own system: they quoted low to win that business last year, then raised prices 20 percent at first renewal, after the tooling was moved and the switching cost was sunk. Their parts also run a 2.6 percent rejection rate.

Joseph's runs 0.4. In nine years, no housing from his shop has ever caused a field failure, and in this industry a field failure is not a service ticket, it is a CDSCO event, a possible recall, a hospital's trust. Requalifying any new supplier for these parts means six to nine months of validation builds and regulatory documentation before the first production unit ships. The flagship pump's order book does not have six to nine quiet months in it.

Quality filed the comparison last quarter without drama: Joseph's housings pass first-inspection at 99.6 percent. The discounter's non-critical parts, the category you already buy from them, clear at 97.4. On paper that gap looks small until someone models what a 0.4 percent field failure rate means when the part wraps a drug delivery channel inside a hospital ward. Your Regulatory Affairs colleague answered that question once in a cross-functional and nobody in the room asked for it again.

So the honest version of your mandate is uglier than the margin note: you cannot really leave, you cannot really pay 18, and the man across the table tomorrow is too experienced to be bluffed about either.

---

**The Pump and the Order Book**

Helix's flagship infusion pump is not a commodity SKU. Gulf tenders for the current generation close in Q3, and production planning already assumes Joseph's housings on the line through December without a supplier change. Tooling for these parts lives in Kochi. Moving it would mean months of engineering time Helix does not have budgeted, plus validation runs that tie up the clean room you share with two other product lines.

That is why "test the market properly" sounds brave in a margin note and expensive in a Gantt chart. Your Procurement Head signed off on the flight, not because he thinks Joseph is bluffing, but because someone has to look the supplier in the eye and find out what the eighteen actually means before finance approves it anyway out of fear of a line stop.

---

**The Math in the Quote**

You spent two evenings rebuilding his price from the outside, because walking in with adjectives would be walking in naked.

Some of the increase is real and checkable. The medical-grade polymer index is up 9 percent year on year; Kerala's industrial power tariff rose; skilled toolroom wages in Kochi have climbed the way they have everywhere. An honest cost build-up supports something in the region of 7 to 9 percent. Which leaves 9 to 11 points of quote that the spreadsheet cannot explain.

One more number from your own quality reports, which you almost missed: last year's design revisions caused three mid-run tooling changes at Joseph's shop, and his team absorbed the rework each time without a change order. Your two process engineers, the ones who ran the rework-reduction project at another supplier in March and took about 5 percent out of that supplier's internal costs, read the reports and said, unprompted, that they could see where Joseph's process is bleeding money that is not his fault.

---

**What the Engineers Actually Said**

You asked them directly, since their offer to help is the one card in your hand that costs the company almost nothing and might mean the most to him: would they actually want to do this again, at another shop, so soon after the last one. Both said yes before you'd finished the question. One of them added, unprompted, that the last project was the first time in two years a supplier's own floor staff had thanked them by name on the way out. That is not the kind of detail that shows up in a savings report, and you are fairly sure it matters more to Joseph than the 5 percent does.

The March project at the other supplier took eleven working days on site. They mapped three rework loops tied to Helix design releases, fixed two without capital spend, and left a one-page standard work sheet the supplier still uses. Finance counted the savings at roughly 5 percent of that supplier's run rate. Joseph's quality logs suggest a similar pattern from last year's revision cycle, except his team never sent a change order and your company never acknowledged the cost.

---

**The Man in Kochi**

Joseph Varghese learned toolmaking from his father, took over the shop at 29, and grew it to 70 people without ever once, as he puts it, "shipping a part I would not sign." He still walks the shop floor himself every morning before the first shift's tea break, a habit his son, who is training to eventually take over, has apparently tried and failed to talk him out of. He is courteous on every call, sends Onam sweets to your office, and has raised prices on Helix exactly twice in nine years, both times with a cost breakdown attached, both times under 5 percent.

Which is why 18 startled you, and why you started asking around. An industry contact told you the story Joseph himself never mentions. Two years ago, a large customer, a consumer appliances brand, pushed him hard on a one-year contract: squeezed the price, promised the relationship, praised the quality. Joseph added machine capacity for their volumes, financed on a five-year loan he is presumably still paying down. At renewal they moved to a cheaper shop in Coimbatore, and the machines he had financed for them ran at half load for fourteen months while he tried to backfill the capacity with other customers. People who know him say he came out of it with a rule he now repeats to his plant manager: "A one-year customer pays one-year prices. Only a partner gets partner prices."

Your predecessor's handover note, which you dug out last week, has one relevant line about him: "Joseph never negotiates against pressure, only against certainty. Give him something he can plan on and he becomes the most reasonable man in Kerala."

One more thing you know from accounts payable, small but real: Helix pays him at 60 days, and his working capital, like every 70-person shop's, is a quiet monthly ache.

---

**What You Carry In**

Your mandate: land the year-one effect at no more than +6. Your authorities, confirmed before the flight: finance has pre-approved multi-year direct-material contracts with indexed escalation, a structure used twice this year in other categories; you can offer the on-site time of the two process engineers for a rework-reduction project at his shop; the second moulded category, Rs 60 lakh currently sitting with the discounter whose renewal behaviour you already know, can be consolidated to a supplier who earns it; and payment terms are negotiable if the numbers justify it.

Finance used indexed escalation on a polymer hose contract in February and a PCB enclosure deal in May. Both were three-year frames with CPI-linked step-ups at renewal instead of open re-quoting. Legal has a standard direct-materials addendum ready; your job in Kochi is commercial, not documentary. The Rs 60 lakh category is pump accessories, not regulated housings, but Joseph's tolerances would cover it and consolidating would give him load on the machines he is still paying for. Whether that volume is a gift or a trade depends on what you learn about why eighteen is the opening number.

On payment terms, Helix's standard is 60 days. Joseph has never asked for early payment in writing, but your AP analyst noted twice last year that his supplier credit line creaked in quarter-end months when three large customers paid late the same week. You have headroom to 45 days if the total package justifies it. You are not authorised to go to 30 without a second signature, and you would rather not spend political capital on terms alone if the price stays wrong.

You have read the margin note again more than once, "or we finally test the market properly," and you no longer think your Procurement Head means it as an instruction so much as a line that sounds decisive in a hallway conversation. Testing the market properly, on this component, means the six-to-nine-month validation clock, and you are fairly sure he knows that as well as you do.

And you remember what the category head who trained you said about anchors, the single most useful thing anyone taught you in four years: "When a good supplier gives you a bad number, the number is a message. Your job is to find out what it says."

You rehearsed the cost build-up on the flight down, not as a script to win, but as proof you respect the inputs he cannot fake. What you do not yet know is what contract shape would let him plan on you the way he once planned on a customer who did not stay.

---

**10 AM, Kochi**

The plant smells of hot polymer and machine oil, and the conference room above the shop floor has a window onto the moulding lines, which you suspect is deliberate: Joseph likes buyers to see what they are buying. A framed photo on the wall shows a much younger Joseph standing next to a single machine that, by the look of the building around it now, was once the entire company. He pours the coffee himself, asks about your flight, and slides a single sheet across the table, his quote, unchanged.

You walk him through your build-up: the polymer index, the power tariff, the wage inflation, the 7-to-9 the math supports, and the counter you are opening at. He listens to all of it without interrupting, nodding at the parts that are true, which is most of it. Then he sets his pen down on the sheet, parallel to the edge, and looks at you without any unkindness at all.

"You have done your homework, and I respect that. The polymer, the power, the wages, yes, all correct." He taps the quote once. "But that number you are proposing, on this contract, the way it is written, that number just isn't possible for us."
