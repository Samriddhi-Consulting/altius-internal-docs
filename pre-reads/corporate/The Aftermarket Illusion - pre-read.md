# The Aftermarket Illusion

It is 8:00 AM on a Friday, and the parts dashboard on your laptop is bleeding red.

You are the Area Parts Manager for the country's leading commercial vehicle manufacturer. Your job is to keep dealer parts volume healthy across the territory. Spare parts are the high-margin lifeblood of the network. When a fleet buys through your dealers, everyone downstream gets paid: the warehouse, the service bay, the field team that tracks availability.

Your biggest account in the territory is Falcon Freight Lines. Vikram Singh runs fleet technical operations there. Fifty trucks on the national corridor. By industry norms, a fleet that size should pull roughly **$200,000** a year in consumables through your dealer network: filters, belts, brake linings, sensors, the parts that keep wheels turning between major overhauls.

This quarter, Falcon's orders through Summit Commercial Vehicles, your exclusive local dealer, have dropped **60 percent**.

You drive to Summit before the morning traffic peaks. Krit, the Dealer Parts and Service Head, is standing in the warehouse aisle between empty pallets. He hands you a printed manifest without preamble.

"Vikram figured it out," Krit says. "He's cut us out. Buying direct from a wholesaler called Global Parts Co."

The manifest is high-volume preventative maintenance: air filters, brake pads, belt kits, the predictable rhythm of a fleet that knows its own service intervals. The line items look familiar until you reach the supplier column. Global Parts Co. on every row. The prices beside them are hard to argue with on paper.

"He called me yesterday," Krit continues. "Found out the air filters and brake pads we sell him are made by a company called ApexTech. Global sells the same ApexTech catalogue for thirty percent less. His exact words: 'It's the exact same part from the exact same factory. Why should I pay a thirty percent markup for a cardboard box?'"

Krit crosses his arms. "I can't match thirty percent. We are losing this account."

---

**The call with your National Parts Director**

You step outside and call your National Parts Director. She has seen this movie before.

"Vikram thinks he's being a genius procurement officer," she says, her voice tight. "He's staring at invoice price. What he doesn't see is the cost of becoming his own parts manager."

"You can't win a price war with a wholesaler," she adds. "Drop thirty percent and you destroy margin and set a precedent for every fleet in the country. Your job is to change how he thinks about maintenance cost, not to match Global's sticker."

When you push back that ApexTech is sometimes the same manufacturer, she doesn't disagree.

"Sometimes it is. Sometimes it's a B-grade reject that failed our internal quality control and left through a side door. That's not the fight you win on the loading dock. You win on what happens after the part is in his warehouse."

She pauses. "Stop talking about the price of metal. Talk about cost per unit of productivity. Vikram's business is moving freight, not counting filter SKUs in a storeroom."

---

**Falcon Freight and Vikram Singh**

Vikram Singh has run technical operations at Falcon for more than six years. Fifty trucks. Uptime, cost per kilometre, and a long memory for vendors who left him holding the wrench.

He is not a cartoon villain. He likes your trucks when they work. He has dealt fairly with your people in better years. He also reports to a board that reads maintenance line items every quarter, and he takes that duty seriously. He respects numbers and homework. He has zero patience for fluff, brand lectures, or "trust the logo."

Your predecessor on this account lasted two years and left for a passenger-car division. The handover note in the CRM was three lines long and did not mention Global Parts Co. You found the leak yourself on a Friday morning, which is either bad luck or the job telling you where to start.

Your field team still talks about **2019**. Eight Falcon trucks failed within eighteen months and ended up in a corner of the yard the drivers call **The Graveyard**. Warranty fights. Dealer runarounds. Vikram has never fully forgiven the network for how that played out, even when the trucks were not strictly a parts issue. Any conversation with him now carries that residue, whether he opens with it or not.

Last month, one of his linehaul units sat grounded for **three days** waiting on a specialised sensor Global had to ship from another state. A **$50** part. Krit's team had tracked the same SKU for Falcon's production cycle and would have had it on the shelf. Vikram's spreadsheet does not include those three days. Your back-of-envelope does: roughly **$600 per day** in lost revenue per grounded truck, and that incident was not the first stock-out his procurement team has logged since they went direct.

Vikram believes he is saving **$60,000** a year. That number is real arithmetic on paper: thirty percent off a **$200,000** annual baseline. He will walk into the room with it highlighted in green. Your job is to learn whether that headline survives contact with how Falcon actually runs.

---

**What self-managed parts inventory costs**

You open your laptop in the car and pull Falcon's operational data alongside what third-party procurement does to a fleet like his.

When Vikram buys through Summit, Krit absorbs obsolete stock when a truck model changes or a part ages out in the warehouse. When Vikram buys direct, that risk lands on Falcon's balance sheet. Heavy-duty fleet benchmarks put **self-managed wastage** at roughly **ten percent** of inventory value per year: obsolescence, damage, theft, the quiet shrinkage of a storeroom nobody audits on Sunday mornings.

Convenience has a price too. Your dealer network gives him one supplier, one invoice, one set of terms. Global Parts Co. is one vendor on a list that has grown since he went direct. His procurement team is now juggling multiple specialised suppliers, separate freight terms, and separate audit trails. Finance put the incremental admin load at roughly **$15,000** a year, and that was before the third-party invoice disputes started showing up in Vikram's inbox.

Availability is the number Krit cares about most. Summit tracks Falcon's production cycles and holds to a **98 percent** parts availability target for contracted fleets. A wholesaler has no obligation to keep Vikram's SKUs warm on a shelf. They fulfil what they have when he orders.

Quality risk is the line your Director returns to when price talk gets loud. If an unvetted third-party part fails and takes an engine with it, the warranty is void. Vikram assumes that risk the moment the box arrives without your traceability chain. He treats "same factory" as a sentence he read on a Global brochure. You have not seen his team verify it.

Finance ran a side comparison last quarter on three fleets that went partially direct. Two of them quietly moved high-value engine components back through authorised channels within six months. The third is still arguing with an insurer over a voided claim on a turbo rebuild. Nobody published that story in a trade magazine. It shows up in whispered calls between area managers instead.

None of this is a speech you deliver in order. It is arithmetic you carry, and questions you ask, depending on what he reveals when you sit down.

---

**The dealer floor**

Krit walks you back through the warehouse one more time before you leave. Empty pallets where Falcon's standing orders used to sit. A service bay calendar with fewer booked slots from Falcon units this month.

"If he signs with Global this afternoon, we lose the rhythm," Krit says. "We stop seeing his failure patterns before he does. That three-day grounding last month? We would have had that sensor. He knows it. He doesn't put it in the spreadsheet."

You ask whether Vikram has mentioned the contract terms. Krit shakes his head. "Year-long supply. Locked pricing. He thinks he won. I think he bought a procurement job he doesn't want."

Your Director texts while you are in the car: **Meeting moved to 2:00 PM. He wants an answer before he signs at 4:00.** The clock is real. Friday afternoon. Year-end budget conversations start Monday.

You think about the last time you sat across from Vikram. It was a routine quarterly review eighteen months ago, before the Global experiment. He asked sharp questions about filter lead times and left happy enough. Something shifted in the months after. Maybe a new procurement analyst with a spreadsheet and a cost-cutting mandate. Maybe Global's sales team found him at the right board meeting. You are about to find out which story he tells himself.

---

**Data you carry into the room**

You will not drop your prices thirty percent. That is not on the table and your Director was explicit.

What you have instead:

- The **$60,000** headline Vikram will wave, and the baseline **$200,000** annual parts spend it assumes
- **Ten percent** wastage on self-managed inventory if his team holds the stock risk
- Roughly **$15,000** in added procurement admin since he went multi-vendor
- **$600 per day** when a truck is grounded, and a recent **three-day** wait on a **$50** sensor
- **98 percent** availability through Summit versus whatever Global publishes on a one-pager
- Warranty void exposure on unvetted parts, and the **2019** history that still shapes how he hears "trust the brand"

You have a folder of calculations. How you use them depends on what Vikram says first, and whether he is willing to test his own assumptions about "same part, same factory."

---

**2:00 PM at Falcon Freight**

At two o'clock you walk into Vikram Singh's office. He does not look angry. He looks like a man who solved a puzzle and wants you to watch him close the lid.

He spins his laptop toward you. At the bottom of the spreadsheet, highlighted in bright green: **$60,000**.

"I like you," Vikram says, leaning back. "And I like your trucks. But I am a businessman. I can buy the exact same OEM-equivalent parts from Global Parts Co. and save sixty thousand dollars a year. Same factory. Just without your logo on the box."

He taps the screen.

"I don't want to sever our parts relationship. But I have a fiduciary duty to keep maintenance costs down. Unless you can match Global's thirty percent discount right now, I am signing a year-long supply contract with them at four o'clock."

He crosses his arms and waits.

You know he is looking at the price of the metal, not the cost of the operation. You have your numbers ready. What you learn in the next hour depends on whether you lead with a discount slide or with questions that make his green highlight earn its place on the page.
