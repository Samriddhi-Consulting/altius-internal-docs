# Too Expensive

Renewals week, and one row on your dashboard has been amber for a month and turned red this morning: Meenakshi's Salon & Studio, Sector 41. Subscription lapses Friday. Auto-renewal switched off, by her, three weeks ago, which among salon partners reads less like an oversight and more like a statement.

You are a Partner Success Executive at the platform, two years in, ninety salon partners in your cluster, and a retention number, 82 against a target of 88, that your team lead has started mentioning with your name attached. There is a fifteen percent discount sitting in your system that you are authorised to use. You have watched what that button does over time. Two desks away sits a colleague who hits his retention target every single year with it, and whose partners now open every renewal conversation with a churn threat, the way regulars learn a slot machine. His average contract value has fallen three years running, and nobody above him has connected the two facts out loud, at least not yet.

Meenakshi Pillai has asked you to come by at three, "to talk before I decide," which from her is already a great deal more warning than most partners bother to give.

---

**The Salon**

Meenakshi's is the kind of business aggregators quietly depend on: eleven years old, two floors, a 4.7 rating across more than eight hundred reviews, an average ticket of Rs 850, and a chair of regulars who have followed her through two locations. There is a framed photo behind her billing counter of the single room she started in, which she has never once taken down despite three rounds of renovation since. She built it from that room, still does the colour work on her oldest clients herself, and knows her cash position the way she knows her own hands. Every partner-success executive who has ever handled this account has left the same note in the system: easy renewal, low touch, never a complaint. This is the first time any of that has been untrue.

Her plan is the Growth tier, Rs 36,000 a year. You pulled the account apart last night and found what the departed rep before you left behind: the core listing-and-booking product is Rs 27,000 of that; the rest is two add-ons, priority placement at Rs 6,000, in a locality where her organic rank is already number one, and a professional photoshoot credit at Rs 3,000, untouched for fourteen months. She has been paying Rs 9,000 a year for nothing, and she does not know it, and neither, until last night, did you.

---

**Her Arithmetic**

On the phone last week she gave you her math, and it was crisp, because her math always is. She had clearly done it before calling, not in anger but the way she probably does every number in that salon, with a pen and an actual piece of paper, twice, to be sure.

"Twenty-eight bookings a month from your app. I counted three months running. That is maybe twenty-four thousand rupees a month, and half those girls would have found us anyway, we are the best-rated salon in the sector. For that I pay thirty-six thousand a year, plus your commission on every prepaid booking on top. Explain to me slowly why."

The commission point stung because it is half fair: the twelve percent on prepaid in-app bookings is real, is separate from the subscription, and arrives tangled together in her head whenever the invoice does.

The twenty-eight is the number you keep returning to, because you pulled something last night from a tab she has apparently never opened. Per the access logs, no one from her salon has ever viewed the analytics section. Her listing's tap-to-call button, the one on every profile, fires about sixty times a month. Sixty people a month find her on the platform and choose to phone instead of book in-app, because that is how half this country still books a haircut. What happens to those calls once they reach her front desk is something you do not yet know in detail. You have the platform-side count. You do not yet know how her register treats them.

---

**The Register You Have Not Seen**

You know how salon registers work in general. Receptionists ask a version of "appointment?" and log yes or no. Walk-ins stay walk-ins. Phone bookings stay phone bookings. Source rarely gets a column unless someone insists on it.

Meenakshi trusts her register the way she trusts her cash drawer. When she says twenty-eight bookings, she means twenty-eight rows that clearly say your app's name. Anything that arrived by phone after someone saw her listing is probably not in that count. Whether that gap matters, and how big it is, is not something the briefing can answer for you. It is something the room has to answer, if you ask the right questions and then stop talking long enough to hear her.

The platform gives her a listing, in-app booking with prepayment, reviews, and that tap-to-call number on every profile. Partners rarely open the analytics that sit behind all of it. That is not a moral failing. It is a pattern. Salon owners live on the floor, not in dashboards.

You are not walking in with a finished equation. You are walking in with a tablet that shows sixty taps a month on a button she may not know she is paying attention to.

---

**What the Fee Might Be Carrying**

You have been doing this long enough to know that when someone who runs her own numbers gets angry at a number, the number is rarely alone.

Three things you have gathered, from the phone call, from her social media, and from the neighbourhood itself. Her rent went up fifteen percent in April; she mentioned it in passing and then waved it away, which is how owners mention the things that keep them up. In March she spent Rs 40,000 on paid social ads, run by her nephew's friend, six redeemed offer visits, an experiment she describes with a laugh that is not one. And eight months ago, two lanes away, Mirror Mirror opened: interiors designed to be photographed, a listing gallery that makes her two floors of genuine excellence look understated, and a spot on the same page her customers scroll. She has walked past it. She has not gone in, and she has told you that fact twice, unprompted, which is its own kind of confession.

"I pay you people thirty-six thousand," she said on the phone, quieter than the rest, "and when my clients open your app, the first thing they see is that place."

You suspect that sentence matters as much as the twenty-eight bookings. The fee may be where every fear this year lives, the way a fever is never really about the thermometer. You do not yet know which fear she will put on the desk today. You know that answering only the price line and never the person behind it is how renewals die quietly.

One more story rides with you, one you must never say out loud in her salon. Two years ago a partner in the next sector churned over price, with math much like hers. Her bookings fell by a third in a quarter, and she returned six months later at full price. Using that story as a threat is the fastest way to lose a proud woman forever. Knowing it is simply knowing what the product does when someone leaves.

---

**Your Team Lead, Briefly**

Your team lead caught you on the way out this morning, not unkindly: "Meenakshi's a big one to lose off a Sector 41 cluster. Use the discount if you need it, that's what it's there for. But get the renewal, that's the number I'm reporting up." What she did not say, because she has said it before and trusts you to remember, is that the discount and the number are not actually the same goal, not for an account like this one, not if you want her still renewing in three years.

---

**What You Carry In**

A tablet with her analytics open to the call data, sixty taps a month, month after month, which she has never seen. Her plan sheet, with the two dead add-ons circled in your own pen: Rs 9,000 a year of priority placement she does not need in a sector where she already ranks first, and a photoshoot credit untouched for fourteen months, both sold by a rep who is no longer here to defend them. The honest split of subscription versus commission, drawn simply, because the tangle serves nobody. A one-month call-tracking proposal you have half-formed, a dedicated tracked number on her listing so anything the register mislabels becomes visible in language she already trusts, reviewed together in thirty days if she wants proof before trust. And the fifteen percent discount button, which exists, and which your team lead mentioned this morning as though it were the obvious answer.

You have rehearsed none of this out loud, on the theory that a rehearsed line is the first thing someone this sharp with numbers spots and distrusts.

Somewhere between the dashboard and the salon door sits the real question, and it is not whether the platform is worth Rs 36,000 in the abstract. It is what the number means to her today, sitting in that back office with the rent notice and the road two lanes away, and she is the only person in the building who can tell you.

Meenakshi is not a line item. She is eleven years of reviews and a woman who turned off auto-renewal three weeks ago instead of ghosting you, which is respect even when it does not feel like it.

---

**3 PM**

The salon smells of ammonia and jasmine, and Meenakshi meets you between clients, foils still on her three o'clock's hair, wiping her hands on a towel with the unhurried authority of someone on her own floor. Through the open door you can hear the wash-basin running and a junior stylist laughing at something too quietly to make out. She takes you to the small back office, moves a carton of colour tubes off the stool for you, and sits with the invoice already on the desk between you, face up, turned your way.

She folds the towel, sets it down, and looks at you with eleven years of knowing exactly what things cost, the same look she probably gives a supplier who has quoted her a price she thinks is a test.

The conversation is about to start on her terms, the invoice already face up between you, turned your way, eleven years of knowing exactly what things cost sitting behind her eyes.
