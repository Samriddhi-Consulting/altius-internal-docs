# Downstream

The phone rang at 10:41 PM.

You were still at the plant. The export bay lights were on. Somewhere beyond the fence a dog barked once and stopped. You had been walking the treatment area with a torch, not because you expected to find anything new, but because sitting in the office made the morning feel closer.

Rajan Verma did not ask if it was a bad time.

"You're in tomorrow," he said. "Board officer. Senior. They're bringing paper."

You knew. The complaint had landed six days ago. Sampling vans. Lab turnaround. The emails from corporate that managed to say urgency without saying anything useful.

"You're going to keep this clean," Rajan said. "Night-shift contractor skipped the step. We terminate the contract. We show the training logs. We show we acted. That is the story."

You waited.

"You understand me?" His voice had the tight calm of a man who had already decided what understanding meant. "Nothing about capacity. Nothing about that memo of yours from February. That upgrade was never funded. You know why. HQ is watching production numbers for this quarter. If they shut us, we miss the order book. People lose shifts. I am not asking you to invent anything. I am asking you not to volunteer a second narrative."

He breathed out through his nose.

"We're aligned on this, right?"

You said you would see him in the morning. He took that as yes.

After he hung up you stood in the dark by the equalisation tank and thought about the memo. Five pages. Your name on the bottom. Dated five months ago. Subject line you can still recite: *Treatment capacity insufficient for current production volumes. Risk of non-compliant discharge under surge load.*

You had sent it to Rajan and copied Engineering. You had attached the utilisation charts. You had written, in one sentence you regretted making so polite, that a rushed order under current headroom was a foreseeable failure mode.

Nobody funded the upgrade. Production kept climbing. The export run six days ago was exactly the surge you had described.

A contractor on night shift skipped a treatment step to keep the line moving. Untreated water went to the drain. A resident downstream noticed the colour and the smell and called someone who called the Board.

That part is true. It is also not the whole of what happened.

---

**Cauvery Coatings**

The plant sits on the industrial spur outside the city, past the truck yards and the stretch where the municipal drain still runs open before it disappears into concrete. Cauvery Coatings makes industrial finishes for metal components. Mid-size. Old enough that the original treatment plant was sized for a quieter decade. Busy enough now that the ETP runs near its design edge on ordinary days and past it when an export bay decides the quarter.

You are the EHS and Compliance Head. You did not invent the night-shift decision. You also did not stop the conditions that made that decision feel, to someone under pressure, like the only way to hit the loading time.

Rajan has run the plant for nine years. He is not a cartoon villain. He is afraid of a shutdown that misses quarterly targets, trips contract penalties, and puts him in front of HQ with nothing but a story about a contractor. That fear is what called you at 10:41.

Corporate wants the plant open. The Board wants a reason to believe the next surge will not look like this one. You are the person in the room who has to speak.

---

**What the Board already has**

You should assume they did not come empty-handed.

Six days is long enough for photographs of discoloured discharge. Long enough for a lab result. Long enough for a draft closure order to be typed, printed, and carried in a folder.

You should also assume they asked around. Plants talk. Vendors talk. Sometimes old internal documents travel further than anyone intends.

You know what you wrote in February. You do not know what she has read.

---

**The numbers you may need**

Consented COD limit for the discharge: **100 mg/L**.

The Board's sampling after the complaint came back at **312 mg/L**. That is not a rounding error. It is more than three times the limit.

Design treatment capacity versus current production: the plant has been running at roughly **140%** of what the ETP was built to handle on sustained volume. Your February memo said as much, with the charts attached.

The export order that drove the night of the bypass was a rush shipment for a buyer who had already paid for air freight on the finished goods. Missing the loading window meant penalties the commercial team had already started calculating in WhatsApp threads you were not on.

Headcount on site across shifts is a little over **420**. A closure does not only stop machines. It stops wages.

None of that tells you what to say. It tells you what is true.

---

**This morning**

You slept badly. You rehearsed versions of Rajan's story and hated how smooth they sounded. You rehearsed versions of the full story and hated how much of it still pointed at decisions you did not control and at a warning you did control and failed to escalate hard enough after it died in a budget cycle.

Security called up at 8:50. A Board vehicle at the gate. One officer. No media.

Rajan caught you outside the conference room. He did not touch your shoulder. He looked at the door.

"Clean," he said. "Contractor. Training. Corrective action on the vendor. You understand me?"

You opened the door.

Meenakshi Iyer is already seated. Senior Officer, State Pollution Control Board. Grey salwar, no theatre. Four stacks of paper on the table, squared so the edges match. She does not stand when you enter. She waits until you sit.

She looks at you the way field inspectors look at a site they have already walked once.

The four stacks of paper are not theatre. They are the six days between complaint and this room. Photographs. Lab sheets. A draft closure you have not seen and must assume exists. Rajan's "clean" story is waiting outside the door like a coat he expects you to put on. The February memo is waiting in your head like a second narrative he told you not to volunteer.

You sit. The chair scrapes once. Somewhere in the plant a compressor kicks on. Downstream, the drain that runs open before it disappears into concrete does not care which story you choose. Meenakshi's pen is already uncapped.

The conversation is about to start.

---

**What loyalty sounds like at 10:41**

Rajan's call was not a conspiracy briefing. It was a scared plant head trying to keep four hundred and twenty people on shifts and an order book intact. That does not make the contractor-only story true. It makes the pressure real. AAA in a classroom becomes something sharper when the person who funded nothing asks you to protect the quarter with your silence.

You are not here to invent a villain. You are here to decide whether "aligned" means telling the Board only the convenient half, or whether truth under pressure includes the capacity memo, the surge you warned about, and the contractor skip that still happened on your watch.

Corporate emails said urgency without saying anything useful. Sampling vans came. Lab turnaround came. Six days is enough for a closure draft. It is also enough for you to rehearse smooth lies and hate yourself for how well they land.

Meenakshi Iyer did not come for a vendor story. She came with paper. Precedents exist for plants that owned the full failure mode and for plants that offered training logs as theatre. You do not know which folder she opened last. You know which memo has your name on the bottom.

The export bay lights were still on when Rajan called. They are off now. The meeting room lights are hard and flat. Your torch from last night is back on the charge rack by the treatment area. None of that gear helps you speak. Only the choice between clean and complete does.

**What you carry that is not on her stacks**

Utilisation charts from February. The polite sentence you regret. The knowledge that night-shift pressure and unfunded capacity are one system, not two unrelated events. The knowledge that terminating a contractor is corrective action and also, if told alone, a narrowing of the truth.

Rajan is still in the corridor. He will hear how this opens. HQ will hear how this closes. Downstream residents already smelled the colour. The Board officer across from you is waiting for your first sentence.

Do not invent facts she does not have. Do not volunteer every chart in the first breath. Do not pretend the contractor skip did not happen. Find the collaborative shift the scenario allows: own what is yours, name what was foreseeable, refuse to let "clean" erase the memo without becoming a lecture.

The pen taps once on the squared paper. She is ready.



The room holds. The clock holds. The next honest sentence is the only lever left in this scene, and it has to be earned rather than performed. You have the facts. You have the pressure. What you do not have is a script that survives contact with the person across from you if you lead with the easy story instead of the true one. Stay with the specifics. Leave room. Do not rescue the conversation with a brochure answer. The meeting starts when they decide you are listening.
---

**Before she speaks**

You can still feel the torch weight from last night in your hand even though the torch is on the rack. The equalisation tank dark. The polite memo sentence. The surge you predicted. The contractor skip you did not personally order and did not personally prevent. Rajan's alignment question. Your non-answer that he took as yes.

Meenakshi's four stacks are squared. Your story is not. That is the work of the next minutes: make the story complete enough to be true without dumping every chart before she asks, and complete enough that ""contractor"" alone cannot erase February. The plant compressor kicks again. Downstream does not wait for your rehearsal to finish.

Six days. Four stacks. One memo with your name. One plant head outside the door asking for clean. One Board officer who has already walked the site in her head. The surge you predicted arrived on a night shift you were not standing. The capacity you flagged was never funded. Both sentences can be true in the same room. Say them like a professional who refuses to let loyalty erase paper.

The pen taps again. Five words of silence left.
