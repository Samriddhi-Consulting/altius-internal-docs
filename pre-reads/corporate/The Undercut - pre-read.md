# The Undercut: An Enterprise Account Manager's Renewal Season

Ramesh still tells the story unprompted, which is usually how you know a story is true.

"Six thirty on a Saturday evening, festive weekend, their busiest hour of their busiest week of the year," he says, and he has told you this before, but he tells it again anyway, the way people do with the one story that changed how they see a client. "Fibre went down at the Kalyan hub. Should have failed over in under a second, that is the entire point of the redundancy path. It just did not. Three hundred stores, every card machine dead, and it is the middle of Diwali week. I remember one woman standing at a till holding a bag of clothes she suddenly could not pay for."

That was twenty-six months ago, a design flaw on your own side, rebuilt since, and it has nothing to do, on paper, with the email sitting in your inbox this morning.

On paper.

---

**Tuesday, 9:40 AM**

The email is only two lines long, and one of them is a courtesy.

"Sharing for transparency ahead of our renewal discussion Thursday. Regards, Kavita."

Attached: a commercial proposal from Lumio Business, addressed to StrideWorks Retail, for enterprise mobility and store connectivity across their full estate. You read it twice. The coverage map checks out. The service catalogue checks out. The number at the bottom is thirty percent below what StrideWorks pays you today.

You are an Enterprise Account Manager at Suvarna Telecom, three years on this desk, twenty-two accounts. StrideWorks, nine hundred footwear stores, Rs 2.2 crore a year, is your fourth-largest. The renewal is in five weeks. Until this morning you would have called it safe, the kind of account you stop thinking about precisely because nothing on it ever goes wrong.

Here is the thing every AM on this account learns eventually: Kavita Krishnan never sends anything she has not already decided what to do with. Not an angry email. Not a warm one. Just facts, arriving early, giving you exactly enough runway to prepare and not one day more. No triumphant subject line. No CC to your manager. No deadline stated anywhere. Somehow that is worse than if there had been one.

---

**What Rs 2.2 Crore Actually Buys**

Thursday will be won or lost on whether you know this account better than the quote does, so you spend the morning making sure.

The critical core: 5,200 connections carrying the stores' POS terminals and broadband. Every card swipe, every bill, every inventory sync in nine hundred shops runs through it. Add the three fibre links into their distribution centres, the arteries everything else hangs off. Then the long tail: about 3,800 SIMs riding in delivery-van trackers, staff devices, warehouse handhelds, the kind of connectivity that matters constantly and not very much, the way stationery matters.

Around the core sits the part that never shows up on a price comparison. A four-hour restoration SLA on critical links. A dedicated NOC line that StrideWorks' own IT team can dial at 2 AM. A named service manager, Ramesh, whom their store-ops head greets by first name, not job title. Ramesh has physically been to their Bhiwandi distribution centre twice this year, once for a scheduled upgrade, once at 1 AM for a fibre fault that never made it into any incident report, because he closed it before it became one.

Lumio's quote covers the same estate on paper. What it structurally cannot cover, because an MVNO rides someone else's network and owns no infrastructure of its own, is that restoration machinery. Their enterprise support is a ticket queue: best-effort, typically twenty-four to forty-eight hours, penalties capped at five percent of a month's billing. None of that is slander, it is their own published terms, and for the van trackers and staff SIMs it is genuinely fine. For the other 5,200 connections, it is a different question entirely.

---

**The Story Ramesh Tells**

You already know the shape of what happened that Saturday, because it is in the file. What you know from Ramesh is what it actually sounded like from inside it.

He was on the emergency bridge call. Three hundred stores, six hours, card machines dead in the middle of the festive season's biggest weekend. The internal estimate that circulated afterward put the lost and abandoned sales near Rs 1.9 crore, for one evening, once. Restoration happened inside the contracted SLA, and the credits were paid in full, which is the kind of sentence that is entirely true and convinces nobody who actually sat through the six hours.

Kavita sat through the six hours. Head of Procurement, named owner of the vendor file, the person whose phone the leadership called every twenty minutes for six hours straight. Ramesh remembers her voice on that bridge more clearly than anyone else's, not because she was the loudest, she was not, but because three hours in, with everyone else starting to repeat themselves from exhaustion, she was still the only person on the call asking a useful question.

"She never once raised her voice at us," he told you. "She just asked, every twenty minutes, like a clock: what's fixed, what isn't. I've thought about that call more than the ones where people actually shouted at me."

She has never once raised her voice about it since, either, in two years of renewals and reviews that followed. Colleagues who know her say that is not the same as having forgotten.

One line in the incident report, which you read properly for the first time this week, has stayed with you since: "Vendor's redundancy design assumed a failure mode that did not match the physical routing at the Bhiwandi corridor." Someone on your side wrote that sentence eighteen months before you inherited this account. Someone on your side also fixed it. Neither fact has ever once been said to Kavita directly, by anyone.

---

**The Part That Is On You**

Two more facts arrived this week through the account grapevine. One arrived by looking in the mirror.

StrideWorks' board has mandated a Rs 5 crore cost-out across the business this year. Telecom is the largest single vendor line on Kavita's sheet, and the CFO has personally seen the Lumio quote. Her savings target is not a negotiating posture, it is her appraisal. Lumio, meanwhile, is doing to retail chains exactly what it did to hotel groups last year: pricing year one for the land grab, genuinely cheaper to run on their asset-light model, betting that enterprise buyers only discover the support gap after they have already switched and it is too late to matter.

The mirror is less flattering. The quarterly business reviews on this account stopped nine months ago, right when your book grew from fourteen accounts to twenty-two and something quietly had to give. Since then the relationship has run on invoices, renewal notices, and a Diwali hamper you did not even pick out yourself. The last real conversation StrideWorks had with anyone on your side, the last time someone showed them what that four-hour SLA had silently absorbed on their behalf, the fibre cuts caught before they became outages, the NOC tickets closed before stores even opened, was three quarters ago. Lumio's quote did not break into this account. It walked straight through a door your own complacency left open, and Thursday's meeting starts with that being true whether you say it out loud or not.

You carry two other stories from the floor, for balance. A colleague matched a twenty-five percent undercut last year to save a logo; the account now re-quotes annually, the margin never came back, and finance mentions it in meetings the way doctors mention a chronic condition, matter-of-fact and slightly resigned. And the one worth remembering on a morning like this: an AM who lost a hospital chain to a cheap rival, exited with the door explicitly left open and no bitterness on the record, and won it back eight months and two outages later at full price, because when the client finally came back, there was no face left for either of them to lose.

Your own manager, when you mentioned the Lumio quote this morning mostly to have it on record, gave you four sentences and a shrug: "Don't match it outright, margin's too thin already this quarter. Don't lose the core either, that account's in three regional QBR decks. Use the new SLA credits if you have to. Your call." Both more trust and less help than you wanted, in roughly equal measure.

---

**What You Carry In**

Freshly authorised this quarter, and you read the enablement note twice to be sure of it: penalty-backed SLAs on critical links, Rs 50,000 service credit per hour of breach, the first time you have been able to put real money behind your own uptime claims. Pricing flexibility to twelve percent. And, new this quarter, permission to price different parts of the estate on different terms instead of quoting the whole book at one number.

The arithmetic, if the room lets you use it: Lumio's saving is about Rs 66 lakh a year. The Saturday cost was put at roughly Rs 1.9 crore, in six hours, once. Somewhere between a four-hour restoration and a next-day ticket sits a number, and the only person whose measure of it counts is the one sitting across the table.

You know this account's shape better than the quote does, and you know what your own authorities now allow. What you have not done, in nine months, is sit in a room with Kavita and find out which of tonight's numbers she is actually weighing. The last page of your notebook is blank. Thursday is where you fill it.

---

**Thursday, 11 AM**

Her office is procurement-tidy. One laptop, one notepad, the Lumio proposal printed and squared on the table, facing you, which is a message before either of you speaks. She thanks you for coming, skips the weather entirely, and turns the notepad to a page with three bullet points you cannot quite read upside down.

"I'll be straightforward, because you've always been straightforward with me." She rests one finger on the Lumio printout. "Their coverage checks out. Our IT ran the pilot SIMs for a month, no complaints. The commercial terms are signed-off ready. And my board has given me a savings number this year that this contract alone gets me a third of the way to."

She sits back, and for a moment, only a moment, she looks less like a procurement head and more like someone remembering a Saturday.

Her finger does not move from the printout. It stays there the way a finger stays on something when a person has already rehearsed both possible endings to this conversation, and is only mildly curious which one you are about to give her.

"They're thirty percent cheaper. Give me one reason not to switch."
