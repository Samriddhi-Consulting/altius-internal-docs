# Outcomes Not Specs

Manish Arora replied to your fourth email with nine words: "Fine. Tuesday, 4 PM, my office, fifteen minutes. No slides."

You are an Account Executive at a marketing automation platform for D2C brands, two years into the job, and Vanaja Naturals has been on your named-account list all quarter. Ayurvedic personal care, built from the founder's mother's kitchen recipes into roughly Rs 12 crore. The kind of story trade publications love. The kind of online store your product was built for.

It is the 27th of the month. You are at 71 percent of quota. Your manager's send-off this morning was the same as always: show the full platform, you never know what lands.

You have watched what lands when you show the full platform. Prospects nod at all twenty-three features, take the trial, and go quiet.

Vanaja took three months to reply to anything. Your first email got no response. Your second, from a different angle, referencing a press feature on sustainable packaging, got nothing. Your third, shorter, got a one-line "not right now." Your fourth worked: four sentences, no adjectives. You have wondered since whether that was the actual lesson, and whether you are about to unlearn it in the room.

---

**The Business**

You did the homework properly this time, and the picture is unusually clear.

Vanaja Naturals sells face oils, hair treatments, and body care at an average order of Rs 1,350. Around 60 percent of revenue, roughly Rs 7.2 crore a year, flows through their own online store. The rest through marketplaces and quick commerce, where margins are thinner and the customer belongs to the platform, not the brand. Their buyers are women, 25 to 45, Tier 1 and 2, and they live in chat. The brand's social presence is polished. Reviews use the word "trust" more than any other.

Ad costs for D2C personal care have climbed about 40 percent in eighteen months. In a podcast clip from March, Manish said it himself: "We used to buy growth. Now we have to earn it from the customers we already have." Retention economics, in other words, which is why owned-store revenue suddenly matters to him.

Two of Vanaja's closest competitors, both smaller ayurvedic D2C brands you have tracked for other deals, built proper retention flows this year. One mentions it in a public investor update. Manish has not said anything about this out loud. A founder who reads his category's investor updates the way most people read the news has almost certainly noticed.

From the store itself, two numbers you pulled with the standard audit tools. The site's abandoned-cart rate runs about 68 percent. Carts worth roughly Rs 30 lakh walk away every month, and no recovery message of any kind follows them. You tested it yourself with three abandoned carts and heard nothing back. Repeat purchase rate, from published cohort chatter and category norms, sits near 22 percent. Good personal-care D2C runs well north of 30. At his volumes, the gap between those numbers and normal is not a rounding error.

---

**The People**

Marketing at Vanaja is one person. Kavya, 26, handles everything: campaigns, the content calendar, influencer coordination, and, by the evidence of send timestamps, a weekly ritual of exporting order lists and hand-blasting undifferentiated emails and chat broadcasts. About fifteen hours of her week. Open rates on those blasts are sliding. Two of the brand's messaging numbers have been flagged for spam, which for a chat-first customer base is like a shop with a jammed front door.

Kavya joined eighteen months ago as a fresher, straight out of a marketing diploma, and has quietly absorbed every task nobody else on a five-person team had time for. Her posts on a professional networking site describe her role with more optimism than the send timestamps suggest she has time to feel. In one, from four months ago, she wrote about wanting to "move from manual campaigns to actual personalisation." Nobody on your side has asked her what stopped her. Nobody on your side has asked to meet her.

You have her first name and a role description. You do not have a meeting with her. That absence is itself information. Every AE who pitched Vanaja this quarter pitched Manish. None of them, as far as your contact knows, asked whether the one person who would actually run the tool could be in the room. Manish notices that pattern. Founders who have been burned by shelfware notice who the AE is selling to: the buyer of the licence, or the operator of the work.

About Manish, two things beyond the founder story. First, the calendar note his assistant added to your invite: "MA has a 4:20 with the contract manufacturer. Please be brief." Second, from a mutual contact at a D2C founders' circle: Manish turned down two SaaS pitches last quarter after fifteen-minute meetings that never became second meetings. Neither rejection came with much explanation beyond not the right fit for us right now. In a small enough industry, that kind of polite no eventually gets decoded. The same contact, when you pressed, said Manish does not hate software. He hates software that needs a team he does not have. What that meant in practice, the contact did not say, and you did not push.

---

**What You Carry**

The standard deck lists twenty-three capabilities. Most will never come up today.

Cart-recovery flows: automated sequences triggered when a checkout is abandoned, configurable in a few hours by one person with no engineering help. Post-purchase journeys: follow-ups after a sale, review requests, replenishment reminders, cross-sell nudges timed to typical repurchase windows. Chat automation: broadcast and one-to-one messaging through the channel Vanaja's customers already live in, with delivery and read-receipt tracking email never gives you. Segmentation on recency, frequency, and order value, so a message to a first-time buyer never looks like a message to someone on their eighth order. Beyond that: campaign analytics, A/B testing, loyalty modules, a referral engine, inventory-aware promotions, and roughly a dozen more, most built for a marketing team larger than one person.

Somewhere in the middle of that list sits a detail that never makes the deck. The spam flags on Kavya's messaging numbers are almost certainly a deliverability problem, not a content problem. The kind a properly configured sending setup fixes in days, not months. Small, unglamorous, and only useful if you know Kavya exists.

Pricing on the rate card: roughly Rs 24,000 a month on annual billing, Rs 29,000 monthly. The right three or four capabilities depend on what Kavya can actually run alone, starting Monday, without an implementation team she does not have.

In your bag: a laptop with the deck you were told to show, a printed one-pager of the full feature list, and a notebook in which, last night, you wrote three numbers. Sixty-eight percent. Twenty-two percent. Fifteen hours. Underneath, a question you have not answered yet: which three features, of the twenty-three, are the only ones this man needs to hear about today.

Also in the bag, metaphorically: the 27th, the 71 percent, and a team story you have heard often enough to know by heart. The colleague who wins renewals shows almost nothing in demos. Three capabilities, deep, each anchored to a number from the customer's own business. Her trial-to-close rate is unremarkable. Her thirteen-month retention is the best anyone has seen. You have never watched her open a deck. Your manager, when someone mentioned it in a team meeting, changed the subject.

You ran the arithmetic yourself last night. Five percent recovery on Rs 30 lakh of monthly abandoned carts is about Rs 1.5 lakh a month against a subscription that is a fraction of that. Two automated flows could return most of Kavya's fifteen manual hours. A three-point lift in repeat purchase at Vanaja's volumes is not a rounding error; it is the kind of annual revenue a founder notices. None of that math belongs in your mouth as a pitch. It belongs in his, if you can get him to do the work of connecting problem to number without you performing the slide for him.

The clock on your phone says 3:58 when you reach the stairs. Fifteen minutes. One interruption already rehearsed in your head. The packing floor noise rises as you climb. You can still choose, in the next ninety seconds of climb, whether the first thing out of your mouth after he stops you is another capability, or a question about abandoned carts, Kavya's hours, and what a good week would look like if the tool actually ran without becoming another login he pays for and ignores.

---

**The Shelfware Scar**

There is one more piece of context your mutual contact eventually gave you, almost as an afterthought. Two years ago Vanaja bought a CRM. It was configured in a burst of optimism, used for about six weeks, and then sat on an annual auto-renew while Kavya kept exporting lists by hand. She still calls it "the thing we pay for the login to." Manish does not lead with that story. He does not need to. The anger in the room when someone starts a feature tour is the story.

That scar is why fifteen minutes is not a generous gift. It is a filter. Founders who have paid for unused seats learn to interrupt early. They also learn that the AE who keeps selling the catalogue after the interruption is not listening. The ones who survive the filter ask about the operator, the payback, and the scar, before they ask for a signature.

The 4:20 manufacturer call is real. So is the packing line downstairs. So is the 27th of the month and your 71 percent. None of that helps you if you open the deck again.

---

**4 PM**

His office sits above the packing floor, and the smell of sandalwood and cardboard follows you up the stairs. Through the window behind his desk you can see the packing line itself, three women taping boxes at a pace that suggests the podcast line about ad costs was not an exaggeration. Manish shakes your hand warmly, moves a carton of samples off the chair for you, and sits with his phone face-down but present. The 4:20 is already pulling at him.

You open, as trained, with the company and the platform. You are perhaps ninety seconds in, somewhere between "journey builder" and "advanced segmentation," when he raises one hand, friendly and final, like a man stopping an auto he does not intend to take. The conversation is about to turn. Your next line will decide whether this is another polite no, or the first honest fifteen minutes he has had with a vendor all quarter. Choose carefully right now.
