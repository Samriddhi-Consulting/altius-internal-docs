# The Conversation Nobody Wants

Ravi Sethi taught you the Okhla beat.

Five years ago, in your first month at Packwell India, you were a trainee handed a territory map and a list of factory gates that did not want to see you. Ravi took you along in his Santro for two weeks. He knew which plant manager drank his chai with no sugar, and which purchase head's son was preparing for JEE. He knew the back gate stayed open past four if you gave the watchman the supervisor's name. You watched him walk into an auto-components plant that had rejected the firm twice and walk out forty minutes later with a trial order, mostly by remembering that the owner's father had started the business out of a single shed in 1987 and asking about it.

Salesman of the year in 2016. Again in 2018. When the Rajview Auto account, then the firm's largest, threatened to leave over a botched delivery in 2019, it was Ravi who drove to their Faridabad plant at 7 AM, sat with their stores team through the reconciliation, and saved it.

Eighteen months ago, the firm made you Area Sales Manager. Six reports in a twenty-person NCR sales team. Ravi Sethi, fifteen years your senior, is one of them. He congratulated you before anyone else did, at your desk, with both hands on your shoulders. You have wondered since what that cost him.

You have managed him for a year and a half without ever once having to say anything he did not want to hear. Praise, approvals, the odd scheduling favour, all easy, all one-directional. This is the first time the job has asked you to be the person in the room he does not want in the room.

It is now the last Thursday of the quarter. He is due in your cabin at 4 PM. On your desk sits a document with his name on the first line and "Performance Improvement Plan" on the second. You have never delivered one before. HR needs his signed acknowledgement by Friday.

---

**The Company That Moved**

Packwell sold corrugated packaging for its first eighteen years. Boxes, done well, delivered on time. A rep who knew his clients and worked his beat could live an entire career inside that catalogue. Many did.

In the last three years the company has changed shape. Two new product lines launched: flexible packaging, and a biodegradable range that pharma and export clients now ask for by name. A packaging-line audit service was added on top, the kind of consultative sale that turns a box vendor into a partner. Five new reps were hired in the last eighteen months, all under thirty, all fluent in the AI-assisted proposal tools the company rolled out last year. Their proposals go out in 48 hours, thirty pages, the client's own volumes modelled in. The old standard was a two-pager in a week.

The market moved the same direction. Three serious competitors now sit in every deal. Clients who once renewed on a handshake now float RFQs annually, and at least one of Ravi's oldest accounts has started doing exactly that, quietly, without telling him.

Inside the team, the arithmetic of effort has quietly inverted. There was a time when 70 percent effort produced 90 percent of target and management was pleased. Now the honest version is the reverse: 100 percent effort produces 80 percent, unless the approach itself gets smarter. Ravi's CRM still looks like someone working that hundred percent every morning. That is part of why this conversation feels unfair before you have opened your mouth.

---

**The Numbers on Your Desk**

Ravi's file, which HR compiled and you have read four times.

Revenue: Rs 1.12 crore this year. Almost exactly what he delivered four years ago. The line is flat. What moved is the bar: three years ago his target was Rs 1.15 crore and he closed at 96 percent. This year the target is Rs 1.5 crore, same as every senior rep, and the same Rs 1.12 crore is 75 percent. Second-lowest in the team.

Effort is not the story. His CRM shows the most client visits in the team and the earliest check-ins logged most mornings. His call notes run longer than anyone else's. Whatever this is, it is not laziness.

His 28 accounts are the oldest book in the company, names that have bought from Packwell for a decade. All 28 buy corrugated. Exactly one has ever bought a second line. The team's attach rate, accounts buying two or more lines, is 30 percent. The new hires run at 45.

Two losses this year stand out. In March, Sharma Auto Components, his account for nine years, asked for a biodegradable packaging proposal for their export line. Ravi submitted the corrugated renewal with a note that the new range details would follow. They signed the sustainable contract with a competitor eleven days later; the corrugated business stayed, for now. In June, a pharma prospect he had warmed for months went cold after a rival's proposal, thirty pages, their volumes modelled, arrived before his two-pager did.

And one line in the file you keep returning to: monthly product reviews and trainings, attendance 2 of the last 9. His stated reason, relayed through a colleague, is that he has sold this product for thirteen years and does not need a classroom. You checked the dates against the HR joining records last night. The missed streak begins the same quarter the first batch of new hires walked in. The two sessions he did attend, both early on, he sat in the back and left before the Q&A.

---

**What the File Does Not Say**

The file does not say that when a client of fifteen years shifted his son's wedding cards order, a personal thing, nothing to do with packaging, he still asked for "Ravi ji." It does not say that two of the new hires quietly route their trickiest client-relationship questions to him, or that he answers them generously, in the corridor, never in the meetings he skips. It does not say that he has never once, in eighteen months, made your job harder than it needed to be, even when yours became the desk that used to be someone else's.

Because here is the part the spreadsheet cannot hold: his book is the richest unworked farm in the region, not a weak book at all. Run the team's own attach rate against it, even partially, a handful of his 28 accounts adding one more line at the usual per-account value, and the incremental number starts looking like real money, well before you get anywhere near all 28. His path back over target does not require a single new logo. It requires him to sell what the firm has become to people who already trust him, which requires him to know what the firm has become, which requires the room he will not enter.

You have the rough math in your head from last night's read. You are not walking in with a finished recovery spreadsheet. You are walking in with a question about whether there is a person in the plan or only HR's deadline.

---

**The Document**

The PIP, drafted by HR, is on your desk for tomorrow's submission. Ninety days. Three commitments: six of his accounts adopting a second product line, full attendance at product reviews and trainings, and proposal turnaround under five days using the standard tools. Review checkpoints at 30, 60, and 90 days.

Your boss, the Regional Sales Head, was not subtle at the quarterly review: "No passengers this year." When you asked, carefully, whether someone else might handle the Ravi conversation, given the history, she looked at you for a moment and said, "He's your report. That is the job."

You also remember Mumbai, last quarter. A manager there delivered a PIP the way you deliver an apology; the rep heard "formality" and changed nothing; the exit two months later was bitter and nearly ended up legal. Both of them, you remember thinking at the time, walked out of that first meeting believing it had gone well. You have rehearsed none of what you might say out loud, on the theory that a rehearsed line is the first thing someone who taught you the Okhla beat will spot and distrust.

The target is not yours to move. Rs 1.5 crore stands, and so does the 90-day window; HR signed off on both and will not reopen either one for a conversation, however it goes. What you do have room on: which of the three commitments Ravi tackles first, whether the trainings happen in the group session or one-on-one with you to start, and how the 30-day checkpoint is framed, a hard stop or a genuine progress check. None of that changes what is being asked of him. All of it changes whether he experiences this as being handed a sentence or being handed a way back. You will need to own the message yourself. "Management decided" will sound like someone else's cabin, and this one is yours now.

Friday is tomorrow. The acknowledgement line on page two still has a blank signature box.

You keep circling the same discomfort, and it is not really about the numbers. Five years ago this man taught you which watchman opened the back gate after four, and which plant owner's father had started the business in a single shed. He put both hands on your shoulders and congratulated you before anyone else did on the day you got the job that made you his manager. Now you are the one holding a document with his name under words nobody wants to read, and the effort logged in his CRM every morning is real, which is exactly what makes it hard to hand him a number that calls it a shortfall. None of that changes what the target is. All of it changes what this half hour costs, for both of you, depending on whether he leaves the cabin feeling quietly written off or handed something he could still do something with.

---

**4 PM**

He knocks once and comes in, the way he has for thirteen years, into a cabin that was somebody else's when he taught you the job. No file in his hands. He has seen the meeting title in the calendar invite; HR requires it be named honestly, and it was. Outside, the sales floor is doing its usual 4 PM noise, someone closing a call too loudly, a printer jamming near the window, all of it carrying on exactly as if nothing in this room is about to matter.

He sits down across from you. He does not reach for the water. For a long moment he looks at the document on your desk, upside down from where he sits, and you find yourself thinking about a Santro in Okhla, and which of the two of you was teaching the other, then.

"Thirteen years," he says, evenly. "So tell me straight. Should I take this as a sign that the company wants me to leave?"
