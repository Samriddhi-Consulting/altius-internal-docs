# The Falcon Ultimatum

The heat rising off the tarmac at Falcon Freight Lines' central national depot is enough to distort the horizon. It is 2:00 PM. The sun is merciless. That is not what makes you sweat.

Tucked in the far corner of the compound, fenced behind chain-link and razor wire, sit eight of your brand's heavy-duty prime movers. Fine dust covers them. Corporate decals peel off the doors. They have not moved in three years.

Internally, Falcon calls this corner **The Graveyard**.

You are the new Area Sales Manager for one of the country's largest commercial vehicle manufacturers. Your territory runs the national freight corridor from the southern coastal ports up to the northern industrial highlands. Falcon Freight Lines is the crown jewel on this route. Fifty new heavy-duty prime movers would lock your quarter. Losing them hands the corridor to Titan Motors.

In 2019, before you took this territory, your predecessor sold Falcon twenty trucks. Spec'd poorly for the 8% gradient highland routes Falcon was expanding into. Within eight months, eight units suffered catastrophic transmission and drivetrain failures. Your company blamed Falcon's drivers. Falcon blamed weak machinery. Eighteen months of warranty fights ended in a bitter stalemate. The dead trucks were dragged to the corner of the lot. A multi-million-dollar monument to broken promises.

Falcon has won a massive national freight contract. They need **50 new heavy-duty prime movers**. Biggest deal of your young career. To win it, you have to walk past The Graveyard every time you come here, and still make a case Vikram Singh will hear.

A yard supervisor nods at you from the weighbridge booth and looks away. Everyone on this lot knows what those eight trucks mean. Drivers still call the fence line by name when they train new hires. You did not put them there. You still have to sell past them.

---

**Amina on the speakerphone**

"I don't care about the trucks from 2019. I care about this quarter."

Amina El-Sayed, your National Sales Director, is already talking when you pull out of the Falcon gate.

"We are missing country-wide volume by ten percent. Country management is not happy. The Falcon order of fifty units is the lifeline for our region. You cannot lose this deal. More importantly, you cannot give away the farm to get it."

"They're going to squeeze us on price," you say, merging onto the highway. "Vikram Singh is leading it. Fleet Technical Director. He's waving a Titan quote in my face."

Amina groans. Titan Motors has been flooding the market with aggressive low-cost imports.

"Titan is undercutting our base price by twenty percent," you continue. "Vikram expects a match, or at least a fifteen percent 'reparations' discount for the Graveyard."

"No massive base-price drops," Amina snaps. "We have margin floors for a reason. Titan trucks are cheap to buy and fall apart in three years. Hold the line. Sell him Total Cost of Ownership. Upsell the premium features. Do the math. Get the fifty units closed by Friday."

The line goes dead. Volume. Margin. Beat Titan. On a spreadsheet it looks clean. In the room it will not.

Your phone buzzes once more before you pocket it. Amina again, short: **Friday. No farm giveaway.** The corridor does not care about your predecessor's warranty file. It cares whether fifty units hit the books before country management opens Monday's review.

---

**Tariq and the service bay**

Your next stop is Summit Commercial Vehicles, your brand's exclusive dealer. Tariq Mansour, Dealer Principal, pours coffee in his office and does not waste time.

"I hear you sit with Vikram tomorrow for the fifty units," he says. "We need this win to keep Titan out of our backyard."

"We do. He's going to be demanding. I may need flexibility on delivery, and maybe some service concessions."

Tariq holds up a hand. "I will handle delivery. I will not budge on one thing. Falcon wants to do their own maintenance on this fleet."

You already know. Vikram's preliminary term sheet asks for open API access to raw CAN-bus and telematics data so Falcon can run third-party parts and its own predictive maintenance. After 2019 downtime waiting on Summit mechanics, he does not trust the dealer bay.

Tariq's face hardens. "If you give them the data keys, I lose service revenue on fifty trucks for the next ten years. That is my cash flow. I will refuse Pre-Delivery Inspection. I will not support the warranty. Lock them into our Premium Predictive Maintenance contract. Sell the AI diagnostics. Protect my service bay."

You leave with a headache. The dealer is your partner. Right now he feels like a second opponent.

In the showroom bay you catch a glimpse of a Falcon unit on a hoist for routine work. A mechanic wipes his hands and shrugs when you ask how the account has felt lately. "He pays. He watches the clock. He does not forget who left him waiting." That is the soft version of what you will hear tomorrow across a boardroom table.

---

**Numbers you carry (not a speech)**

Back in the hotel room at midnight, you open the dossiers. Vikram is demanding **13-liter, 520 horsepower engines with heavy-duty hub-reduction rear axles**. Fear talking. He knows Titan cannot match that big a powertrain, so he wants the beast spec at a steep discount to "match" Titan's sticker.

Your pitch is the newer **11-liter, 460 horsepower powertrain with direct-drive single-reduction axles**. Lighter. More efficient. Better retarder. You have to prove it on Total Cost of Ownership, not on brand loyalty.

Falcon's own fleet data, per truck, sits beside your engineering sheet:

- Your brand base price: **$120,000**
- Titan's quote: **$96,000** (exactly twenty percent under)
- Annual distance: **100,000 km**
- Diesel: **$1.10** per liter
- Extra payload revenue: **$40** per extra ton per trip, **200** trips a year
- Unscheduled downtime: **$600** per day

On the 13L hub-reduction unit Vikram wants: about **3.2 km per liter**, PM every **40,000 km** at **$800**, one extra steer-tire set a year at **$1,200**, brake linings every **50,000 km** at **$1,000**, roughly **four days** unscheduled downtime a year.

On your 11L direct-drive pitch: about **3.8 km per liter**, PM every **50,000 km** at **$750**, no tire penalty, advanced hydraulic retarder stretching brake replacements to every **100,000 km**, about **two days** downtime, and **350 kg** lighter so Falcon can haul **0.35 extra tons** of paying cargo every trip.

On Titan's 12L "Value Hauler": **$96,000** day-one savings of **$24,000** per truck versus your base, about **3.4 km per liter**, PM every **30,000 km** at **$600**, weak retarders forcing brake work every **40,000 km** at **$800**, and a reputation for electrical failures averaging **fourteen days** of unscheduled downtime a year.

You run the annual operating stack (fuel, maintenance, tires, brakes, downtime) for each path. Vikram will wave Titan's twenty percent. Your job is to make the year-three bleed visible without reading him a spreadsheet like a script.

You sketch the highland corridor on a hotel notepad: port exit, Checkpoint grades, the long climb where 2019 transmissions cooked. The 13L looks like insurance on that climb. The 11L looks like money left on the table unless you can show fuel, retarder, and payload doing real work on those exact grades. Titan looks cheap until fourteen downtime days a year eat the sticker savings. None of that lands if you open with a slide titled "Why We're Better."

---

**Driver churn and the Premium Cab**

Cold coffee. One more file. Industry intelligence on Falcon's internal operations.

**Driver turnover** is the red flag. Falcon needs **60 drivers** to keep fifty new trucks on shifts. Current annual turnover sits at **fifty percent**. Thirty drivers replaced every year at roughly **$6,000** each (recruiting, onboarding, training, idle truck time). The fleet is old, loud, and punishing for a ten-hour day. Drivers leave for competitors who offer a better cab.

Premium Cab upgrade: **$2,000** one-time per truck. Your projection: turnover drops to **twenty percent** (twelve replacements a year). Titan offers zero ergonomic options. Fleets running Titan stay near **fifty-five percent** churn on ride quality alone.

Amina forbade the fifteen percent flat discount Vikram wants. You have TCO math and driver economics instead. None of it is a close you recite in order. It is arithmetic you carry, and questions you ask, depending on what he opens with.

You remember a quiet note from a Falcon dispatcher at last quarter's ride-along: two senior drivers threatened to leave if the next buy was "another bone-shaker." Vikram did not put that in the RFP. He put engine displacement and discount percent. Your job is to find whether cab comfort and churn cost are live for him, or whether he will bat them away until uptime and price are settled.

---

**Vikram Singh**

Vikram has run fleet technical operations at Falcon for more than six years. Fifty trucks on the corridor. Uptime, cost per kilometre, and a long memory for vendors who left him holding the wrench.

He is not a cartoon villain. He likes your trucks when they work. He has dealt fairly with your people in better years. He also reports to a board that reads fleet CapEx every quarter, and he takes that duty seriously. He respects numbers and homework. He has zero patience for fluff, brand lectures, or "trust us this time."

Your predecessor sold him the 2019 batch and left the territory. The CRM handover was thin. You inherited The Graveyard and a man who still walks past those eight dust-covered units on the way to the boardroom. Last quarter he sat through a Titan demo day at a highland rest stop and came back with a red folder. He has not signed. He has not forgotten either.

People who have worked with him longer than you say the same thing in different words: he will pay for what works, and he will punish anyone who sells him hope. He still buys filters and booked PM through Summit when the trucks are healthy. That is not loyalty. That is habit under protest. Habit breaks when a red folder looks cheaper than another year of waiting on a dealer bay.

Friday is still Amina's clock. Volume targets do not move because you need another discovery call. Somewhere between Amina's margin floor and Tariq's service bay, there is a deal shape that keeps fifty units on your brand without handing Vikram the raw keys. You do not walk in with that shape printed. You walk in ready to earn it.

---

**Friday morning at Falcon HQ**

At 9:00 AM you walk through the glass doors of Falcon Freight Lines' corporate headquarters. A sterile, freezing boardroom. Through the window you can see the yard, and the dust-covered shapes of the 2019 Graveyard in the distance.

The door opens. Vikram Singh walks in. He does not smile. He drops two thick folders on the table. One bears your company's logo. The other is bright red, Titan Motors. He crosses his arms. He looks like a man who has already decided, and is daring you to change the decision.

You have the TCO stack, the Titan comparison, the Premium Cab churn math, Tariq's hard line on open API and raw telemetry, and Amina's Friday mandate with no farm giveaway. How you use any of it depends on what he says first.
