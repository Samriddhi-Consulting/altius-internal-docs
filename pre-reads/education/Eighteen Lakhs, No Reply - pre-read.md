# Eighteen Lakhs, No Reply

The cheque came back on a Tuesday.

Not with a letter. Not with a call. With a stamp and a slip and the particular silence that follows when a bank has already decided what it thinks of you. Arjun Mehta stood at the counter of a branch that smelled of floor polish and wet umbrellas, and watched the clerk slide the instrument back across the glass as if returning a book that had never been interesting.

Eighteen lakhs. Drawn on an account that had, three weeks earlier, looked ordinary enough to trust.

He did not raise his voice. He asked once whether there had been a clerical error. There had not. He folded the slip into the same leather folder he had brought the cheque in, thanked the clerk, and walked out into afternoon heat that made the pavement soft.

On the footpath he unlocked his phone and called Vikram Sood.

It rang. It rang again. Then it went somewhere that was not voicemail and not a person. The line simply stopped being a line.

He called twice more from the cab. Same result. By the time the driver asked which gate, Arjun had already decided he would not leave another message. Messages were for people who still believed the other side was listening.

That night he sat at the dining table after his wife had gone to bed and lined up every document he could find: the dissolution agreement, the cheque copy, the bank slip, the courier receipt for the notice he would send the next morning. He squared the edges until the stack looked like something a competent man would bring to a lawyer. Competence, he had decided, was the only story he was willing to tell in public.

---

**The partnership that looked clean**

Six years earlier, in a rented office above a stationery shop in Lower Parel, the story had sounded different.

Arjun had the capital and the discipline. Vikram had the introductions and the voltage. Clients who would not take a meeting from a spreadsheet took a meeting from Vikram. Arjun watched him work a room the way some men watch a good batsman: with professional envy and private gratitude. He had vouched for Vikram at their previous company. He had brought him into this one. For two years the venture did what ventures are supposed to do when trust is cheap and work is expensive. It grew.

Then, somewhere in year three, the growth developed a limp Arjun could not name.

Vikram started missing the weekly numbers call and calling it travel. Client dinners happened without Arjun, then without notes afterward. A procurement head who used to copy both of them began copying only Vikram. When Arjun asked, Vikram smiled the smile of a man who had already prepared the answer. *You handle the engine. I handle the road.*

It was almost convincing.

By the time Arjun understood what the road had been for, Vikram had already registered something of his own. Soft launch. Soft poaching. Soft enough that nobody in their shared circle used the word theft. They used words like *transition* and *bandwidth* and *it was time.*

The last partners' meeting lasted eleven minutes. No raised voices. Vikram brought a printed schedule for handing over client relationships and a draft dissolution that looked like it had been written by someone who slept well. Arjun signed because the alternative was a war he did not know how to fight in public, and because the cheque was supposed to make the ending look like commerce rather than injury.

The dissolution agreement was tidy. Lawyers on both sides. Signatures that looked like adulthood. And a cheque for eighteen lakhs that was supposed to close the account the way a door closes when you mean it.

The door did not close. It bounced.

---

**The notice, and the nothing after**

The legal notice went out with a courier who wanted a signature and got one. The receipt went into the folder under a blue tab. Arjun waited the way people wait when they still believe process has a personality.

Nothing came back.

No call. No lawyer's letter. No half-apology dressed as a scheduling conflict. Vikram's silence was not the silence of a man who had forgotten. It was the silence of a man who had finished with a chapter and closed the book.

In the weeks that followed, Arjun kept running into the edges of the story in places that were not supposed to hurt. A WhatsApp group where someone asked, lightly, whether Vikram was "building something new." A dinner where a mutual acquaintance said Vikram seemed well, and then changed the subject too quickly. Arjun smiled in the places smiling was required. He did not correct anyone. Correction would have required a version of events he was not ready to hand over.

He told his wife the matter was in process. He did not tell her how the process felt when you were the only one inside it.

**What the file on Ananya's desk says**

You are a junior associate at Kapoor Desai & Associates. Partner Ananya Kapoor was meant to take this meeting. At 10:41 she messaged the associate channel from a corridor outside Courtroom 12:

> Stuck. Matter listed out of turn. Arjun Mehta is at 11:00. Conference Room B. Folder is on my desk under the red clip. Do not make him wait in reception. I will join if I can. If I cannot, take instructions and do not invent comfort.

You have fourteen minutes.

The folder under the red clip is organized the way people organize things when they are trying not to feel anything. Tabs. Dates. A printed timeline in Arjun's handwriting that does not wobble.

The file you carry (lookup facts, not a ledger to dump): cheque for **₹18,00,000** drawn by Vikram Sood as a dissolution payment after a six-year venture. Bank return: dishonoured, stamp and slip on file. Legal notice issued with courier receipt attached. No response. Client ask: file. Immediately.

There is also a one-line note in Ananya's hand on a sticky at the top:

*Referred by R. Malhotra. Client is composed. Do not confuse composure with simplicity.*

You do not know Malhotra beyond the name on a referral email that says only: *Arjun is solid. Treat him properly. This is not a fishing expedition.* You do know the firm does not hand first meetings to juniors unless something has gone sideways in the diary. Today something has gone sideways in the diary.

---

**The man who arrives early**

At 10:58 the receptionist buzzes Conference Room B.

Arjun Mehta is forty-six and dressed like a man who has decided the world will not see him sweat. Dark jacket. Open collar. Shoes that have been cleaned recently. He carries the folder you already know by description, and when he sits he places it on the table and squares it so the edge faces you, as if alignment itself were a kind of argument.

He looks at the empty chair where Ananya should be.

"She was delayed," you say, because that is the truth and because lying about seniority is a short road to a long problem.

He nods once. Not warm. Not hostile. The nod of a man recalculating the room.

"I was told she understands commercial disputes," he says.

"She does. I'm covering until she can join. I've read the file."

He considers that. Somewhere outside, a printer starts and stops. The air conditioning makes the kind of noise that becomes silence if you live with it long enough.

"All right," he says. "Then we should not waste time."

He opens the folder. You see the cheque copy first, then the bank slip, then the notice. Everything is where it should be. Everything is ready. The readiness is the point.

He has the look of someone who has rehearsed an opening for days.

---

**What you can see from here**

You can see a bounced instrument and a notice that went unanswered. You can see a dissolution that was supposed to be clean. You can see a client who speaks in short sentences and keeps his hands still.

You can also see, if you are paying attention, that he says "my former partner" the way some people say the weather: as if the phrase were small, and as if daring you to treat it as small.

You cannot see what he has not told his wife. You cannot see what his circle has started asking quietly about Vikram's absence. You cannot see what he wants this process to do to the man who wrote the cheque, beyond the rupees on the face of it.

Ananya's sticky note sits in your memory like a tripwire.

*Do not confuse composure with simplicity.*

The window for certain procedural options is already narrowing. That much is in the timeline. Urgency is real. Urgency is also a trap if you let it turn the meeting into a filing desk with a human attached.

Arjun closes the folder halfway, then opens it again, as if reminding himself what he came to do.

He looks at you.

He is ready to begin.



---

**What the Folder Will Not Say**

Eighteen lakhs is the number on the cheque. The humiliation is the number that does not print. Ananya is delayed; you are covering; Arjun expected a partner and got a junior associate. The courier receipt proves notice went out. Nothing in the folder proves what he wants Vikram to feel. That part only surfaces if you treat ""former partner"" as a person instead of a caption under a bounced instrument. The conference room clock is quiet. His urgency is not.

Ananya's delay is not your failure, but it is your opening condition. Arjun arrived on time with a squared folder and a demand to file. The bounced cheque is real. The public humiliation want is also real, and it conflicts with privacy and speed in ways he has not named yet. If you stay on documents, he will stay on documents. If you pause on former partner, the consult can become the conversation he actually booked without knowing how to ask for it. The reception clock keeps moving either way.

Eighteen lakhs changed hands between men who used to be partners, and the only paper that outlived the friendship is the cheque that bounced and the courier receipt proving notice went out. Arjun did not come only to recover money. He came because someone he trusted made him look like a fool in front of people who mattered, and no folder has a column for that. Ananya is still delayed. Vikram has not said what outcome he wants, only how quickly he wants it, and reception has booked this room again in forty minutes.
