# Transferred Twice: An Escalations Specialist's 11:40 AM

The board shows him waiting before you hear him: priority queue, third contact, sentiment flag red, account age six years.

You are a Senior Escalations Specialist at the firm, five years in support, two on this desk, which exists for exactly one kind of call: the ones the first two tries didn't fix. The desk comes with authorities the frontline doesn't have, a goodwill wallet of Rs 2,500 on your own signature, the power to file mandate suspensions and priority field tickets directly, refund filing up to Rs 5,000 once the paperwork allows, and a direct callback line you may give out when you judge it worth giving. It also comes with a truth the training deck never quite says: by the time a call reaches this desk, the customer is no longer angry about their issue. They are angry about the company's handling of it, and those are different repairs.

Suresh Iyer, the screen says. Coimbatore. You have ninety seconds of queue time to read six years of account history, and what you find in it makes you want the ninety seconds back.

Six years, and until three months ago, not one call. The kind of account that never shows up in a team meeting because there is nothing to say about it. He paid, the service worked, nobody thought about him, which in this business is the highest compliment an account can receive without anyone meaning to pay it.

---

**What the Account Shows**

He did everything right, in order, with reference numbers. Twenty-six years running a school's administrative office teaches a particular kind of patience with paperwork, and a particular intolerance for anyone who treats a reference number as optional detail rather than the whole point of keeping one.

On 14 March he called to cancel his Pune home broadband, relocation, a school administrator moving cities for a posting. The cancellation request was logged; he was given a reference, which the call note shows he read back to the associate, twice, to confirm. He moved on the 20th.

On 2 April, his account was debited Rs 1,177. On 9 April he called: the associate checked, saw the cancellation request sitting in the CRM, and told him, in good faith and in error, that the debit was "a processing lag" and would auto-reverse. Ticket closed: resolved. On 2 May, the debit fired again. He called on the 6th: this associate, also correct within her screen, explained that refunds are owned by finance, raised an escalation ticket, and promised a callback within 48 hours.

The callback never went, and the reason is the part you read twice. The ticket landed in your desk's queue, sat unactioned through a heavy week, and was auto-closed by the 72-hour no-action rule, the queue-hygiene rule your own team runs, the one you defended at a team meeting in February with the words "a stale ticket is worse than no ticket." You remember saying it. You remember people nodding. Nobody in that room, including you, asked what happens to the customer attached to the ticket that goes stale. On 2 June, the debit fired a third time. A chat attempt on 28 May got a scripted apology and a link.

Three debits. Rs 3,531. Two explanations, two closed tickets, one promised callback that your desk's own machinery quietly ate.

---

**Where the Account Actually Is**

It takes you three screens to find the root, and when you find it, it is nobody and everybody.

The cancellation request exists, billing can see it. But at the firm, cancellation completes only when operations closes the account, and operations closes the account only after the router-recovery visit, and the work order for that visit, created correctly on 14 March, was never scheduled: the field team's app filters out orders once a plan status reads "cancellation requested," a filter built years ago to hide noise, hiding, in this case, the one order that mattered. So the account never closed. Because it never closed, the auto-debit mandate stayed live at the bank, and the debits are, mechanically, the system working perfectly. And because there is no closure date, finance's refund macro returns, accurately, "no closure date on account," which is why the refund has gone nowhere and will continue to go nowhere until someone moves three departments in the right order.

Associate one was right that the cancellation sat in the system, and associate two was right that finance owns refunds. Both of them were also missing the same thing: deeper still, operations had never closed the account at all. Everyone right, individually, and a six-year customer debited for a service he cancelled a quarter ago, in a city he no longer lives in.

Nothing moves without a closure date, and the closure date does not exist until a router visit happens at an address he no longer lives at. The durations underneath are real, and none of them are yours to shrink: the bank side of a mandate runs seven working days, a field visit carries a 48-hour SLA, finance's refund cycle is seven to ten days. Which of those clocks you are willing to start today, and how plainly you tell him what each one costs, is a decision the queue timer will not make for you.

---

**What the Desk Knows**

Two people on this floor have taught you this job, one by example and one by warning, and you did not choose either lesson on purpose. Both simply happened to sit near you.

The warning sits four seats down: the desk's fastest talker, whose call-resolution numbers lead the board every month, and whose method is a promise, "your refund will be processed within 24 hours, sir," delivered warmly, sincerely, and impossibly. The refunds take their ten days regardless. The customers call back on day three, angrier, and land on other headsets, and the desk's repeat-escalation rate, the number nobody prints on the wall, is his true output.

The example sits two seats away and has for three years: a specialist whose name appears, spelled correctly, in NPS comments, a thing the platform wasn't designed to encourage. She has the second-lowest call-resolution speed on the floor and the lowest repeat-escalation rate by a wide margin, a pairing nobody above her pay grade has quite figured out how to put in a performance review. You have never quite reverse-engineered how she does it. From the outside it is only a shape: she is slower on the clock than almost anyone, she promises less than the fast-talker four seats down, and the customers she handles do not come back. Whatever it is, it is not speed and it is not charm. You have wondered, more than once, what she says on those calls that you do not.

One more line from the account before the call connects, a note field on the 6 May ticket, typed by the associate, presumably from something he said: "Cust mentioned consumer forum if not resolved. Polite throughout."

Polite throughout. Somehow that is the detail that lands.

---

**Your Authorities**

What you can actually do on this call, without waiting on a manager:

Goodwill credit or waiver up to Rs 2,500 on your own signature. File the mandate suspension directly: seven working days, bank-side, cannot be compressed, must be said honestly. Raise a priority field-ops ticket with a 48-hour SLA for the router visit at his old Pune address. File the refund for all three debits, up to Rs 5,000, without finance sign-off, once a closure date exists on the account. Give out the direct callback line when you judge it worth giving, perhaps twice a month, and you are already fairly sure you will give it today. Mark the ticket specialist-owned, which exempts it from the auto-close rule, your rule, the one that ate his callback.

What you cannot do: compress the bank mandate timeline, refund before closure date, or make finance move faster than their seven-to-ten-day cycle. Those limits are fixed. Whether he hears them hidden or said plainly is not.

---

**What You Carry In**

You carry in the whole history, read, so he will not be asked to tell it a third time; the root cause, which you understand well enough to say in one honest sentence that blames the gap and not a name; the real durations, including the seven working days you cannot shrink and will have to decide whether to hide; the goodwill wallet; the specialist-owned flag, which exempts a ticket from the auto-close rule, your rule, the one that ate his callback; and the direct line, which you give out perhaps twice a month, and are already fairly sure you will give today.

What you do not carry in is a script. You know this account better than anyone who has touched it in three months. Whether that knowledge reaches him as a person who has been failed three times, or as one more competent explanation from one more voice on this company's phone lines, is the only thing the next ten minutes actually test.

---

**One Other Thing, Briefly**

Meenal, who runs the desk, flagged this call to you personally before it connected, not because it needed her attention but because it needed yours: "Third contact, six-year account, sentiment red. Don't let this become a case study in the wrong direction." She did not say what the right direction was. She has watched you handle harder calls than this one, and the not-saying was its own kind of trust.

---

**What Suresh Is Protecting**

He is not shouting. He has never shouted on any of the three contacts. He is a man who keeps reference numbers the way other people keep photographs, because paperwork is how you prove you did your part when the other party did not.

What he needs before he believes a refund timeline: one person who takes ownership out loud and proves it with something he can verify. The consumer forum complaint on his desk is not rage. It is the next item on a list he has already organised. Two promised callbacks that never came taught him that words on this company's phone lines are cheap. He will measure you against writing, dates, and the first update that arrives when you said it would.

The consumer forum, if it surfaces, is not a threat he hurls so much as a step he has already half-completed, in order, like everything else he does. How you meet that, whether it rattles you into promises or not, will tell him more than any apology can.

---

**11:42 AM**

The call connects. A beat of ambient quiet, a school corridor somewhere behind him, a bell or a whistle in the far distance, and then a voice that is not shouting, has never shouted, and has organised itself the way a man organises something he expects to need in writing later.

He has reference FLK-3319 from March 14th. He has the dates of three debits since. He has what your colleague said on April 9th and what your colleague said on May 6th. He has yesterday's chat link to the page he was already on.

He has explained this twice. The question he will ask is simple, and it is the only question that matters until you answer it honestly: are you actually going to help him or not?

The headset waits. The account is open on your screen. The pad has the sequence sketched. The goodwill wallet is one click away. The specialist-owned flag is right there in the ticket menu, the same menu that let his last ticket vanish into queue hygiene.

You will not get a fourth chance to hear him for the first time.
