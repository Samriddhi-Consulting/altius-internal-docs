## The Core Idea

Your students pass exams. They can explain the SPIN selling framework in an assignment. They can list the stages of negotiation in a viva. They can define active listening in a multiple-choice test.

Can they actually do any of it?

Put them in front of a sceptical customer, a defensive employee, a tough procurement head, or an anxious patient, and you'll find out quickly. Most of them freeze, default to textbook language, or skip straight to a solution without understanding the problem. Not because they weren't taught well. Because they were never asked to *practise* the conversation in a setting that felt real.

This is the gap between knowing and doing. It exists in every programme that teaches interpersonal and professional skills. And it's remarkably hard to close with the tools most institutions currently have.

**Altius** closes it. Students practise realistic professional conversations with a persona that adapts to what they say, how they say it, and what they leave out. After each conversation, they receive a detailed, behaviour-level coaching debrief. Not a grade. A mirror.

The simulation *is* the assignment. The debrief *is* the assessment. And the transcript is the kind of evidence that accreditation panels actually want to see.

## Five Problems Altius Solves

### 1. The OBE Compliance Gap

NEP 2020, NAAC, and NBA all mandate Outcome-Based Education. Every programme must define Course Outcomes and Programme Outcomes, and demonstrate that students have attained them.

For technical subjects, this works reasonably well. You can test whether a student can solve a differential equation or write a financial model.

But what about outcomes like these?

> *"Students will be able to conduct a consultative sales conversation that uncovers customer needs and handles objections effectively."*

> *"Students will demonstrate the ability to deliver constructive feedback in a professional setting."*

> *"Students will negotiate effectively in multi-party, multi-issue scenarios."*

How do you assess these? Currently, most institutions use written exams, presentations, or group assignments. These test whether students can *describe* consultative selling. They don't test whether students can *do* it when a sceptical customer is sitting across the table.

Altius gives you a direct assessment of the doing. Each student's simulation produces per-criterion behaviour scores that map directly to Course Outcomes. The percentage of students above a threshold becomes your CO attainment metric. Auditable, timestamped, individual.

### 2. The Case Method Ceiling

The case method is brilliant for developing analytical thinking. It is limited for developing conversational competence.

A case study is one-directional. The student reads, analyses, and presents. But the customer in the case doesn't push back. The CEO doesn't get defensive. The vendor doesn't change their position based on what the student says. The case teaches students to think *about* a conversation. It doesn't teach them to *have* one.

Altius is what happens when the case study talks back. The student reads a contextual briefing (like a case), then walks into a live conversation with an AI persona who responds differently depending on what the student does. Ask good questions, the persona opens up. Pitch too early, the persona shuts down. Show empathy, the persona engages. Ignore the context, the persona calls you out.

This is not a replacement for cases. It's the layer that comes after: the part where students stop analysing the decision and start making it.

### 3. The Faculty Bandwidth Problem

A faculty member teaching 60 students cannot run individual role-plays with each one. The maths doesn't work. Even at 10 minutes per student, that's 10 hours of one-on-one practice, plus feedback time. For a single exercise. In a single course.

Group role-plays are the usual workaround, but they have their own problems. Only two students practise at a time while the rest watch. The "customer" is another student who doesn't know how to push back convincingly. And the social dynamics of the classroom mean students play it safe. They perform for the room, not for the learning.

Altius scales the best possible counterpart to every student, individually, simultaneously. Sixty students can run the same simulation in the same week, each getting a private, adaptive, 20-minute conversation and a detailed coaching debrief. The faculty member reviews the aggregate data, identifies patterns ("most of the batch struggled with probing depth"), and uses that to design the next class session. Less time grading. More time teaching.

### 4. The Employability Evidence Problem

Placement cells hear it every year. Recruiters say: "Your students know theory but can't hold a conversation." HR managers from recruiting companies say: "They're strong on frameworks, weak on application."

This is not a reflection of poor teaching. It's a reflection of what gets practised and what doesn't. Students spend hundreds of hours preparing for exams. They spend almost no time practising the conversations they'll have in their first job: a discovery call, a client meeting, a feedback session, a negotiation.

NAAC Criterion 5 (Student Support and Progression) and NBA Programme Outcomes both require evidence of professional competence. Altius debrief data is that evidence. Not a supervisor's subjective rating from a summer internship, but a structured, scored, transcript-backed assessment of what the student actually did in a realistic professional interaction.

### 5. The Experiential Learning Documentation Gap

Every Self-Study Report claims the institution practises experiential learning. Industry visits. Live projects. Guest lectures. Internships. Simulations.

But when accreditation panels dig into the evidence, it's often thin. Attendance logs. Project cover pages. Supervisor sign-off letters. Company certificates. These document *participation*. They don't document *competence*.

Altius generates a different kind of evidence. For every student interaction, there is a full conversation transcript, per-criterion behaviour scores, qualitative coaching feedback, and a timestamp. This is the kind of artefact that demonstrates experiential learning happened *and* produced a measurable outcome. It sits between an exam and an internship, with the rigour of the first and the realism of the second.

## What Altius Does

The student experience has four steps.

### Step 1: The Briefing

The student receives a scenario link. They read a contextual briefing, the way a professional would prepare before a client meeting or a difficult conversation.

> *"You are a FlowBridge account executive. Kabir Mehta, the CPO at Gulab & Glow, is dealing with a customs crisis. His Diwali launch is 17 days away. He agreed to an async Slack chat for one hour. He's distracted, guarded, and not interested in your pitch. Your job is discovery, not selling."*

The briefing sets the scene, provides background data, and establishes what the student needs to figure out through the conversation itself. It ends at the exact moment the chat begins.

### Step 2: The Conversation

The student chats with an AI persona built for that scenario. The persona has a name, a role, a backstory, hidden concerns, and emotional responses that shift based on what the student does.

This is not a chatbot that responds to keywords. The AI adapts continuously. If the student acknowledges the crisis and asks about root causes, Kabir opens up about the real operational mess: version-control chaos, files scattered across five platforms, 70% of his time on firefighting. If the student ignores the crisis and starts pitching, Kabir stays closed. "I'm literally dealing with customs right now. Can we talk about what actually happened first?"

The conversation runs for 15 to 25 exchanges. Long enough to develop a real dynamic. Short enough to simulate realistic time pressure.

### Step 3: The Coaching Debrief

Immediately after the conversation ends, Altius generates a structured coaching evaluation.

| What's assessed | What it looks like |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Strengths** | "Acknowledged the crisis before asking questions. Used Kabir's language ('version-control hell') to reflect understanding." |
| **Areas to improve** | "Did not explore the cost of the relabelling incident. Missed the opportunity to quantify recurring operational waste." |
| **Per-criterion scores** | Crisis-Appropriate Tone: 4/5 · Needs Discovery: 3/5 · Probing Depth: 2/5 · Stakeholder Awareness: 3/5 |
| **Overall assessment** | "Good instincts on rapport. Needs significant work on moving from symptom to root cause." |

The rubric is custom-designed for each scenario. The evaluator persona (e.g., "expert B2B discovery coach") is calibrated to assess the specific competences the course is trying to develop.

### Step 4: The Faculty Dashboard

The faculty member or programme director sees operational progress.

- Which students have completed the simulation and which haven't.
- Full transcripts. You can read exactly what any student said and how the AI responded.
- The scored coaching debrief is not in the faculty dashboard. Faculty or a programme specialist delivers it from the Altius analysis.
- Cohort patterns still land for the programme. "32 out of 58 students scored below 3 on probing depth" is a concrete input for your next lecture, from that analysis. Accreditation artefacts (transcripts, criterion scores, cohort summaries) come from the same path.

**Patient Communication (Medical)**

> A medical intern must deliver a diabetes diagnosis to a 45-year-old patient who came in for a routine check-up and was not expecting bad news. The patient is in denial, asks if it's "just borderline," and wants to know if they can manage it "with diet alone." Altius evaluates the quality of the communication, not the clinical content. The medical knowledge is the student's. The simulation tests whether they can convey it with clarity and care.

**A note on professional disciplines.** For law, medical, and similar fields, Altius develops the conversational and relational competence that sits alongside domain expertise. It evaluates how students communicate, not whether what they communicated was professionally accurate. The AI persona reacts as a real client or patient would: emotionally, humanly, sometimes irrationally. It does not assess or respond to domain-specific reasoning. This makes Altius complementary to existing programme assessments. Faculty remain the authority on domain accuracy. Altius gives them a structured, scalable way to assess whether students can apply that knowledge in a human interaction.

## How We Set This Up

This is the faculty path from brief to accreditation-ready artefacts. (The four steps above are the student session.)

1. **Brief.** You name the conversation students must be able to hold, the counterpart, and the competences or Course Outcomes you need to assess. What does a strong student do that a weak one doesn't?
2. **Design.** We build the persona, the student briefing, and a rubric mapped to those outcomes. Days, not a semester project.
3. **Review.** Faculty play-test and sign off. In regulated fields (law, medicine), you remain the authority on professional accuracy; Altius assesses how students communicate under pressure. Live in one to two weeks from a clear brief.
4. **Cohort.** We invite the batch by email. Magic link. Browser on phone or laptop. No app. No LMS required.
5. **Run.** Students practise asynchronously: a full batch of 30 to 60 in the same week, privately, without 60 live role-plays. You see completion and can open any transcript.
6. **Debrief & evidence.** Faculty or a programme specialist delivers the coaching debrief from the Altius analysis. You keep timestamped transcripts, criterion scores, and cohort patterns you can file for IQAC, CO/PO attainment, or AoL: not just attendance logs.

A typical first run is one batch of 30 to 60 on a single scenario.
