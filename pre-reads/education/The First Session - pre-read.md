# The First Session

The referral note fits on one screen if you do not zoom in. You read it twice on the way to Room 214 anyway, the way you do when nobody is watching and the hallway is long enough for doubt to catch up.

You are a psychologist, a few months into your first job. The degree is done. The internship hours are logged. The license is real. What is not real yet is the confidence that is supposed to arrive with the paperwork. Most of your sessions so far had someone nearby: a senior colleague down the hall, a case discussion after, a nod that said you handled it adequately even when you were not sure you did.

Today's referral does not come with that. You are walking into a hospital ward on your own. No one watching. No one to hand this off to if it goes sideways.

Your supervisor, Dr. Mehta, signed off on the consult list this morning with her usual half-sentence of encouragement: "First solo ward referral. Keep it simple. You are not there to diagnose. You are there to see if he will come back." Simple is doing a lot of work in that sentence.

---

**The Referral**

Mr. Anil Deshmukh, forty-four, site supervisor at a construction company. Eleven days ago a scaffolding platform gave way under him. He fell just over four meters. Fractured pelvis and right ankle. He has been in the orthopedic ward since.

Physically, he is recovering on schedule. That is not what prompted the referral.

The orthopedic surgeon flagged it in the file: Mr. Deshmukh has gone quiet. Not literally silent. He answers when spoken to. He has stopped asking about his recovery timeline. He has been short with his wife on her visits. He skipped two physiotherapy sessions, telling the physio he "didn't see the point today."

Nobody has pushed him on it directly. The surgeon's note simply says the patient seems to be carrying something and recommends a psych consult. No diagnosis. An observation.

Mr. Deshmukh did not ask for this. He agreed to it the way anyone agrees to another item on a hospital schedule.

The ward nurse who walked you to the corridor mentioned, quietly, that he has been "difficult" with physio, which in ward language usually means silent rather than abusive. The orthopedic registrar said, on the stairs, that the bones are doing their job and something else is not. Neither offered a theory. Both sounded relieved to hand the next hour to psychology.

---

**Who You Are Meeting**

You do not know much beyond the file, and the file is thin on the inside of a person.

What you do know: this is a man used to being the one in charge. Site supervisors run rooms. They walk sites with a clipboard and a voice people answer to. Eleven days of depending on nurses to help him to the bathroom is not a role he occupies comfortably.

He has no history of therapy or counseling. In his world, sitting with a stranger to talk about feelings may not register as a familiar or valued use of an hour. You are, to him, an unknown quantity. And a young one, at that.

You do not yet know what he replays at 3 AM. Whether it is the fall itself, or something about what it means for his job, his team, his sense of himself. You are not here today to find out all of it. You are here to see if he will let you back in the room tomorrow.

That is a smaller goal than it sounds. In a first session with a guarded, non-treatment-seeking patient, smaller is often the only honest win.

Anil Deshmukh built his team over eight years on a commercial site that runs six days a week. He knows which foreman will cut a corner and which will not. He has fired people and hired them back when they earned it. He is the person who gets called when a pour is going wrong at dusk and everyone else wants to go home. Eleven days in a bed with a bell he hates pressing has removed every lever he trusts.

His wife has visited most evenings. The chart note says visits have gotten shorter. The nurses say he barely looked up last time. You do not know yet whether he is protecting her or punishing her or both. You know that pushing him to name feelings about the fall today will likely produce one-word answers and a closed door tomorrow.

---

**What a First Session Is Not**

There is no version of today where you get the full story. Reaching for it too early will very likely close the door rather than open it.

If he questions whether you are qualified, the instinct is to list your training, your placements, your license. Credentials rarely earn trust in a bed with a leg brace. Steadiness does.

If the ward feels slow and his answers stay short, the instinct is to fill the space: another question, a reassurance, a gentle push toward the accident because that is what the referral is about. The referral is about your hypothesis. His pace is about his safety.

If he mentions his wife, his job, his team, or being stuck in a bed without naming the fall, the instinct is to steer back to the scaffold because that is the obvious wound. The obvious wound is often the last thing a proud, frightened man will hand a stranger on day one.

You are not here to fix anything today. You are here to be the first person in eleven days who did not need something from him and did not try to manage him.

---

**What Moves the Room (Without Naming It)**

Your training has words for what works in a first encounter like this, and the ward is exactly the wrong place to say any of them out loud. Whatever you learned about first sessions, Anil did not learn it, did not ask for it, and will read the performance of it as one more professional who wants something from him.

He is going to test you first, because testing is close to the only control he has left in a bed he cannot get out of on his own. How you sit with that in the opening minute, and whether you need him to make it easy for you, will tell him most of what he wants to know about whether you are safe to have in the room.

Somewhere in the hour, if it goes well, he may put something small on the table that is not the fall and is not stated as fear, mentioned as though it does not matter. Whether you even notice it, and what you do in the ten seconds after, is the session. The rest is waiting well, and not reaching for a story he has not offered you.

It helps to hold what the quiet in the chart probably is. Eleven days ago Anil ran a site: he decided who worked where, which corner could not be cut, who got called when a pour went wrong at dusk. Now he presses a bell to be helped to a bathroom and waits for someone to answer it. The surgeon reads that as a man carrying something. From inside the bed it is simpler and worse. Every lever he ever trusted to prove he was competent has been taken away at once, in front of his wife, his team, and a ward full of strangers who keep needing things from him. You are the next stranger through the door. He has spent eleven days being managed, and he will notice, faster than you expect, whether you are one more person here to manage him. The one card you hold that none of the others did is that for this hour, you do not actually need anything from him.

---

**The Ward Before You Knock**

Room 214 is on the east corridor. Daytime noise: trolley wheels, a television in another bay, a physio explaining an exercise to someone who does not want to hear it. The orthopedic ward smells like disinfectant and canteen tea.

You stand at the doorframe a beat before knocking. Inside, the curtain around the bed is pulled halfway. Mr. Deshmukh is propped up, leg in a brace, phone face-down on the tray table like he was looking at it and stopped. He does not look injured in a way that explains the quiet in the chart. He looks like a man doing math in his head.

He looks up when you knock.

The first line will not be a question you asked.

"You're the psych person."

He will look you over. He will ask how old you are. He will say you look barely out of college. He will ask if you have even done this before, talked to someone like him.

He will not wait long for an answer before deciding what kind of hour this is going to be.

The brace on his leg is visible from the door. The phone face-down on the tray is a small tell of its own: a man who was looking at something, maybe a message from site, maybe a photo, and decided mid-scroll that none of it was worth finishing in front of a stranger.

You have eleven days of chart and eleven minutes of hallway. He has eleven days of being the patient in the room. The asymmetry is the whole scene.

---

**What You Carry In**

You carry in restraint. Brief steadiness under challenge. Permission for him to be guarded without you performing concern to compensate. Attention to edges: wife, job, team, bed, boredom, pride.

You carry in the knowledge that success today is a slightly opened door, not a breakthrough. That a dry joke or a neutral "come by tomorrow if you want" is a strong close from a man like this.

You carry in the awareness that every other professional in his eleven days here has wanted something from him: the surgeon wanting compliance, the physio wanting effort, the nurses wanting him to ring the bell when he would rather suffer quietly than admit need. Your only useful difference is that you are not, today, asking him to perform recovery on your schedule.

You carry in twenty turns before debrief. When the conversation ends, you will hear how the session actually landed from the other side of the room.

The east corridor is busy enough that nobody is watching how long you stand at the door. That helps. So does the fact that if this goes poorly, you can walk back to Dr. Mehta with a honest account rather than a heroic one. She respects honest accounts.

You knock once on the doorframe. He looks up.
