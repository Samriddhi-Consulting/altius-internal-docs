# The Nordic Standard

---

**The Week Before Your Meeting**

On Tuesday morning, the third vendor in as many days shows Kristian Solberg a portfolio.

The firm is good at what they do. The slides are sharp. The project lead, a polished man in his early forties who has clearly done this hundreds of times, opens with a photograph of a GCC they delivered in Whitefield eighteen months ago. Marble floors. Exposed brick. A café lounge with pendant lights and a living wall. The space looks like the kind of office a founder puts on Instagram.

Kristian waits. He has a question. He has been writing it down at the bottom of his notepad all week, underneath the names of firms and the times of meetings, because he wants to make sure he actually asks it every time.

"What caused it to run late?"

The project lead looks up. "Sorry, this specific project?"

"Any project. What typically causes a fit-out like this to run behind schedule?"

A pause. A warm, practiced smile. "We have very strong project management processes. Our track record across the last four years has been..."

Kristian thanks him. Shakes his hand. Walks out.

He has been in Bangalore for three weeks. He arrived from Oslo on a six-month mandate with a legal pad, a habit of early mornings, and a very specific question that nobody has answered yet. He has sat through five vendor meetings now, three of them in the last three days. Five portfolios. Five assurances. Five handshakes. He goes back to his hotel room each evening and adds to his list of things he still does not know.

The list is getting long.

**The Email**

Your manager finds the inquiry on Tuesday afternoon and forwards it to you with a single line: *take a look at this one.*

The original message is seventeen words. No subject line, no preamble, no vendor form or pre-qualification questionnaire.

*"Inquiry. GCC fit-out. 150 seats. Bangalore. Six months. Please confirm availability."*

A Norwegian mobile number. An email address ending in .no.

He calls you over to his desk. "Veritas Nordic AS," he says, turning the screen toward you. "Norwegian. Professional services and B2B software. Oslo headquarters, been around twenty-seven years, about twelve hundred people across Europe. Clients in financial services, maritime, some energy." He scrolls through a press release from six months ago about their India expansion. "First office here. Never done this before."

"Which means they don't know what they don't know."

"Exactly." He spins the chair back to face you. "Which means whoever sits across from them first and actually helps them understand this market is very hard to dislodge. The other two firms I know have been approached have already been in." He pauses. "They haven't closed anyone. Go find out why."

You have the meeting tomorrow morning. The contact's name is Kristian Solberg. His title is Head of People and Workplace.

---

**Veritas Nordic**

The company has been building things slowly and well for twenty-seven years, and it has no intention of changing that approach in India.

The founding thesis was simple: professional services and enterprise software should be sold with long relationships and zero drama. Veritas Nordic's clients in Norway's financial sector and maritime industry are not looking for transformation partners. They are looking for vendors who are still there in year ten, who write contracts that hold up, who do not surprise them. That ethos has produced a firm that grows carefully, expands only when the foundation is solid, and will not move until it is sure.

The India decision took two years of internal debate. Three members of the executive team visited Bangalore and Hyderabad over eighteen months, each returning with a version of the same conclusion: the engineering talent was there, the infrastructure had improved, the cost model made sense. What delayed the approval was not a belief that India was wrong, but a determination to be certain that Veritas Nordic could do it right. The board eventually approved in Q4 last year with one condition that went unspoken but understood by everyone in the room: this office has to work.

Not just open. Work.

The people sitting in this GCC will be Veritas Nordic's engineering and analytics capability in Asia. They will be on calls with Oslo every morning. Their output will appear in quarterly board presentations. When the Oslo leadership explains to their clients in Oslo, Bergen, and Amsterdam that Veritas Nordic has expanded its delivery capacity, these are the people they will be talking about. The first sixty employees are already confirmed. Several of them have been in Oslo on calibration visits. They know what a Veritas Nordic office feels like.

If the space is wrong, or the launch slips, or those first sixty engineers walk into a building on Day 1 that does not feel like a serious workplace, the India strategy begins on the wrong foot. The leadership in Oslo has been told it will be ready and right. Kristian Solberg's name is on that commitment.

---

**What 150 Seats Actually Means**

There is a persistent confusion in the Indian GCC market between a facility that can seat 150 people and a facility designed for the kind of work 150 people at a firm like Veritas Nordic need to do.

The confusion is understandable. A 150-seat GCC for a business process operation looks different from a 150-seat GCC for a product engineering team, and the design assumptions embedded in each are genuinely different. In the first, density is an efficiency lever. In the second, it is a risk. Engineers doing complex, focused work require acoustic separation, reliable natural light, and the ability to concentrate for long stretches without the audio environment of a trading floor. They also require spaces to collaborate that are not just glass-walled meeting rooms.

This is not a minor aesthetic point. Firms that have lost GCC talent in Bangalore in the last three years consistently identify workspace quality as a retention variable that did not appear in their hiring calculations. Engineers from Oslo who arrive in Bangalore expecting a replica of the workplace they left, and find instead a dense floor with drop-ceiling fluorescents and partitioned hotdesks, do not stay long. They go to firms that understood what they were building.

Veritas Nordic has 60 confirmed for Day 1. The 150-seat target is the 12-month arrival number. The 300-seat target follows over the next two years, which means the design brief cannot be optimised for today. It has to absorb growth without a gut renovation. It has to be designed once and designed well.

The budget exists. The number has not been shared with any vendor yet.

**Kristian Solberg**

He is forty-one years old. He joined Veritas Nordic twelve years ago and has never worked east of Warsaw until this assignment.

He is Head of People and Workplace, which is a title that sounds soft until you understand what it means at a firm like Veritas Nordic. He decides what kind of company Veritas Nordic is to work for. He controls offices, culture programs, the physical experience of the workday. He thinks about these things the way a CFO thinks about cost structure: not as an aesthetic preference, but as a professional conviction with measurable consequences.

His first major office expansion was Amsterdam in 2017. It went wrong, and not in a way he has forgotten. He arrived thinking the contractor had the permit timeline under control. They did not, in the way that mattered. By the time the project closed, he understood the difference between a contractor who manages a process and one who is accountable for it. He has not approached a fit-out the same way since.

Warsaw in 2020 was different. He had done the homework. He spoke to three independent sources about Polish construction permit timelines before he signed anything. He built eight weeks of contingency into a twenty-four-week plan and documented why. The project delivered in nineteen weeks. He considers it, quietly, one of the better things he has ever managed.

India is new territory. He knows it. He arrived in Bangalore three weeks ago with his legal pad, the same one he uses for every expansion project, and began his standard process: read everything, talk to everyone, and ask one question in every meeting until someone gives him an answer that sounds true.

He is not alarmed by the unfamiliarity. He has been in unfamiliar markets before. What alarms him, mildly, is the pattern he has noticed in five vendor conversations. Every firm says yes. Nobody says yes, but. Nobody says yes, unless. Nobody says here is the thing that could cause this to go sideways. In Amsterdam he had not known to ask that question. In Warsaw he had learned to ask it. Here, so far, he is asking it and getting the same polished non-answer.

He has been adding to his legal pad every evening.

**The Competition**

You are not the only firm he is meeting. Your manager has some intelligence on who else is in contention, though it is incomplete.

One is a large pan-India firm with a strong enterprise GCC track record. They have delivered twenty or more GCC projects across Bangalore and Hyderabad in the last six years. Their process is well-documented, their project management is reliable, and their pitch is built around scale and systems. Kristian met them in the first week. The meeting lasted forty-five minutes. He did not schedule a follow-up.

The second is a smaller boutique that recently completed a European pharmaceutical company's GCC on the Outer Ring Road. The relationship was built by the firm's principal over two years before the project began. They are in contention because of that relationship history and the fact that the pharma GCC is, by most accounts, genuinely good work.

Kristian has not committed to anyone. Your manager's read is that the other conversations have produced polished presentations and nothing more.

"He's still looking," your manager said, the morning before your meeting. "Go find out what he's actually trying to build."

---

**The Norwegian Dimension**

This is not a thing to overcorrect for. Kristian Solberg is a professional who has worked internationally his entire career. He knows that India and Norway are different. He is not expecting you to be Norwegian. He is not expecting formal distance or any particular cultural ritual. He is expecting a professional conversation.

What that means in practice is worth understanding.

Norwegian business culture operates on what communication researchers call a low-context model: what is said is what is meant, and what is not said does not exist. There is no reading between lines, because lines are not written in the spaces between words. If someone is uncomfortable with a proposal, they say so. If a timeline is not achievable, they say so. If a number does not add up, they ask about the number. It is a way of conducting business that assumes the other person can handle the actual information, and treats anything less as a waste of both parties' time.

The counterpart of that directness is an intense scepticism toward performed confidence. There is a Norwegian social concept, old and deeply embedded, that translates roughly as: do not consider yourself more important or more capable than you are, and do not present yourself as something you have not earned. It is not modesty exactly. It is a preference for demonstrated credibility over asserted credibility. Titles impress less than thinking. Portfolios impress less than questions. A firm that shows up with a case study answers is less interesting than a firm that shows up with the right question.

Kristian has now sat through five conversations with firms that led with asserted credibility. He has been in Bangalore for three weeks. His patience for it is not infinite.

**What You Bring In**

Your firm has delivered fourteen GCC fit-outs in Bangalore in the last four years. You know the market. You know where the variables are, and you know which variables most clients do not ask about until they become problems.

Bangalore GCC numbers you carry (lookup only; do not dump as a deck): standard fit-out runs about **₹1,400 to ₹2,000 per sq ft**; premium or international spec about **₹2,200 to ₹3,000 per sq ft**. Shell to handover for 10,000 to 15,000 sq ft is typically **18 to 24 weeks**. The critical path gate from design-freeze to procurement lock is **3 to 4 weeks**. Custom furniture lead time is **12 to 16 weeks**. Bangalore permit timelines are genuinely unpredictable at **3 to 8 weeks**.

Standard GCC density in India runs at sixty-five to eighty square feet per seat. European and Scandinavian workplaces often run considerably higher. The gap between those two numbers, applied to a 150-seat project, produces a meaningfully different answer in both square footage required and total fit-out cost. Whether that gap matters for this client depends on what this client is actually planning to build, which is why it is the first thing worth understanding.

The critical path gate is the part that trips most clients. The window from design-freeze to procurement lock is three to four weeks. After that, custom furniture carries a twelve to sixteen week lead time that cannot be compressed by paying more. AV and structured cabling have their own sequencing requirements. Permit timelines in Bangalore are genuinely unpredictable: the honest range is three to eight weeks, and vendors who quote the short end without a specific basis for it are giving you a number that feels good rather than a number that is accurate.

The fastest your firm has turned around a comparable fit-out is sixteen weeks: a building where the shell was completely ready, no MEP changes were required, and the client had approved the design brief within one week of the first meeting. The average from design-freeze to handover is twenty weeks. The project that ran to twenty-six did so because permits on a Whitefield site cleared eight weeks late and nobody had placed the furniture orders on the assumption that they might clear early.

The firms that go far with clients like Veritas Nordic are rarely the ones who said the right things. They are the ones who asked the right questions.

**The Room**

Ten AM. A conference room in your firm's Indiranagar office.

Kristian is already there when you arrive. He is writing in a yellow legal pad. He puts the pen down when you walk in, stands, shakes your hand. Firm and brief. He does not ask about traffic or the weather or how long you have been with the firm.

He sits back down. The legal pad goes face-down on the table.

He looks at you. The conversation is about to start.
