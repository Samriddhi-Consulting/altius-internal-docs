# The Ten Minutes

The ward goes quiet at night in the particular way hospitals never actually go quiet. Monitors beep softly. Footsteps pass and fade. The hush makes every sound feel louder than it is, including your own breathing while you walk the corridor toward Bed 6.

You are a B.Sc. Nursing student on clinical posting, on night shift in the post-cardiac care unit. Fewer hands than dayshift, more ground to cover, and a gap between "everything is fine" and "something is wrong" that can close faster than anyone admits in orientation. You have been on this ward three weeks. You know where the call bells are. You know which beds need extra checks. You also know, from watching the senior nurses, that nights run on borrowed time: one emergency two floors up can pull staff away and leave the rest of the floor holding the line alone.

Ten minutes ago, something went wrong in Bed 6. You are the one walking into what is left of it.

---

**The Ward Tonight**

Your preceptor for nights this week is Priya, a staff nurse who has been on post-cardiac for four years and who does not waste words before 3 AM. At handover she flagged Bed 6 as stable, wife staying, no concerns. That was ninety minutes ago.

Since then the floor lost two nurses to an emergency on orthopedics two levels down. You felt it in the way the station phone stopped stopping: fewer footsteps in the corridor, longer gaps between room checks, the particular tension of a ward that is still functioning but stretched thin. You were charting in the nurses' station when you heard Sunita Rao's voice, not shouting, which somehow made it worse, asking where the nurse for Bed 6 was.

You did not see the middle of the crisis. You saw the aftermath: Priya moving fast with the crash cart already handled, oxygen adjusted, senior physician notified and already en route if needed, then stable. Priya's face in the corridor afterward was the face of someone who knows the next problem is not clinical. It is the wife.

She caught your eye. "Go sit with her. He is okay for now. She is not. Just stay. Do not make it worse."

That is the whole instruction. It is also the whole job for the next twenty minutes.

---

**Mr. Rao**

Mr. Rao is sixty-four. Two days post-cardiac procedure. Recovering well, stable on paper, nothing in his chart that should have made tonight dangerous. His wife, Sunita, has been staying by the bedside most nights, dozing in the plastic chair, the way spouses do when home feels too far away to be useful.

At around 1:50 AM his breathing changed. Sunita noticed before anything on the monitor flagged it clearly: labored breaths, a grayish look to his face, a confusion in his eyes that was not there an hour ago. She pressed the call bell.

No one came.

She pressed it again. Still nothing. The ward was short-handed tonight. Two staff members had been pulled to help with an emergency two floors down. She got up herself and went to find someone at the nurses' station.

By the time a senior nurse reached Bed 6, several minutes had passed. Mr. Rao has since been stabilized. Vitals coming back toward normal. Oxygen support adjusted. The immediate danger has passed for tonight. He is going to be okay on this shift, as far as anyone can say at 2 AM.

But for those minutes, his wife did not know that. She thought she might lose him, and no one came when she called.

The senior nurse is still with Mr. Rao now, finishing the checks. On your way past, she asked you to go sit with Mrs. Rao for a few minutes. She is shaken. Someone should be with her. That someone is you.

Mr. Rao himself has been a quiet patient until tonight: cooperative with obs, asking sensible questions about recovery, grateful in the restrained way older men often are on cardiac wards. Sunita has been the visible one. She remembers every nurse's name by day two. She brings fruit nobody eats and stays past visiting hours because going home to an empty flat felt worse than the plastic chair.

The chart will show a respiratory change logged at 1:58 AM. Sunita will not care about the timestamp format. She will care about the minutes before someone walked through the curtain.

---

**Who You Are Walking Toward**

Sunita Rao is fifty-eight. She has been essentially living at this hospital for two days: vending machine coffee, short naps in a chair, yesterday's clothes still on her body. Under any other circumstances she is warm, even easy to talk to. You would not know that from the corridor. You will not see it in the first minute.

Right now none of that warmth is available. She is still running on the adrenaline of the last ten minutes, the kind of fear that does not switch off because the danger has passed. That fear has nowhere to go except out, and you are the person standing in front of her.

She is not wrong to be angry. Something did go wrong tonight. Whatever happens in this conversation, that fact does not change.

You are not necessarily the nurse who missed the bell. You are the face of the hospital in this moment. That is enough for her to start there.

On a calmer day you might have met the other Sunita: the one who joked with the ward aide about the vending machine coffee, who thanked a junior nurse for adjusting a pillow without making a speech about it. That version is still in the room somewhere. You will not meet her by asking her to calm down. You may meet her by letting the anger be true and staying anyway.

---

**What You Know About the Delay**

You know the outline because the senior nurse said it in one sentence while handing you off: call bell unanswered for several minutes, short staffing, patient declining, wife at the station, team responded, patient stable now.

You do not know whose fault it was. You may never know the full chain on one night shift. You do not need to know that to be honest about what she experienced: the bell did not bring someone quickly enough, and that should not have happened.

What you must not do in the room is disappear behind explanations she will hear as excuses. "We were short-staffed" is true and insufficient. "That's not really my area" is true and cruel. "He's fine now" may be clinically accurate and emotionally beside the point until she feels heard.

What you must not do is make promises you cannot keep. If she asks whether this will happen again, the urge to say "never" to calm her down is strong. It is also empty. She will hear empty.

What you must not do is take on improper liability: scripted over-apology that sounds like a legal statement, or admitting fault for a chain you did not see. Honesty and over-confession are different repairs. She needs the first, not a performance of the second.

---

**What the Night Has Already Cost Her**

You have not lived her ten minutes. It helps to hold the shape of them anyway.

She pressed the bell. She watched his face change. She pressed again. She listened to the ward stay quiet in the wrong way. She made the choice to leave his bedside to find a human being because the system she was told to trust did not answer. She does not know, minute by minute, what "several minutes" means when your husband is barely breathing in a bed you cannot leave unattended without guilt.

When she asks "why didn't anyone come," she is also asking something she may not say until later: "Was I about to lose him alone?"

Anger is what shows first. Fear is what sits under it. Your job is not to talk her out of being upset. It is to stay steady enough that she can be upset at a person who is telling the truth.

Orientation covered disclosure in abstract terms: be honest, do not speculate beyond your knowledge, do not promise what the system cannot deliver. It did not cover standing in front of a wife who had to leave her husband's bedside to find help. The gap between policy language and a person in yesterday's clothes is where this conversation will be won or lost.

---

**Two Questions, Not One**

There are two questions in this scene, and they do not arrive in the same voice.

The first comes immediately, hot and legitimate: why did nobody come when we called. The second may come later, and only if something in the first few minutes earns it: is this going to happen again. That second question is the fear that has been sitting under the anger the whole time, and it is fragile. It closes again fast if she decides you are performing rather than being straight with her. Which of the two you are actually answering, at any given moment, is worth knowing even while she is still talking.

The clock is still past 2 AM. The ward is still short-handed. You have twenty turns before debrief, and she has already waited longer than she should have had to for anyone to sit down and look her in the eye.

---

**The Room You Are Entering**

Bed 6. The curtain is half-drawn. Mr. Rao is resting now, oxygen line in place, monitor showing steady numbers in the green the way monitors do when the crisis has passed and the room has not yet caught up.

Sunita is standing beside the bed, not sitting. Arms crossed. Hands not quite steady. She turns as you approach.

The first question will come low, shaking, not soft.

"Where were you?"

She is not finished when you answer. She will ask why nobody came when they called. That accusation is the entry point. Another question may come later, only if something in your tone lets it: whether this will happen again. That one is not a challenge in the same voice as the first. It is the vulnerable question underneath, and it only lands honestly if she believes you were straight with her first.

There is no line that fixes this instantly. The goal is narrower and harder: that she leaves this conversation feeling like at least one person in this hospital was honest with her tonight.

---

**What You Carry In**

You carry in presence, not a script, which is harder than it sounds at 2 AM on a short-staffed ward. You carry in the fact that something did go wrong tonight, and that she is not wrong to be angry about it. And you carry in the difference, which orientation named but never made you feel, between being honest with her and performing an apology at her, between staying inside the discomfort and reaching for a staffing chart to explain it away.

You carry in twenty turns before debrief. When the conversation ends, you will hear how it felt to be on the other side of your words.

The senior nurse is still at the bedside behind the curtain. The ward is still short-handed. The clock is still past 2 AM. Mr. Rao is stable. Sunita Rao is not, not yet, and she does not owe you calm she does not have.

You are at the curtain now. She is looking at you.
