# The Meltdown

It is 6:00 AM on a Tuesday, peak of the national freight season, when your phone vibrates off the nightstand.

You are the Area Service Manager for the country's leading commercial vehicle manufacturer. Your job is to keep trucks on the critical national freight corridor moving. When wheels stop, customers lose money. When customers lose money, they call you.

You answer. Vikram Singh. Fleet Technical Director, Falcon Freight Lines. No greeting.

"Four of my trucks are dead in your dealer's yard," he says. "They lost power on the northern highland incline. Limp mode. My cargo is sitting there rotting. Your dealer is refusing to touch them, claiming it is not a warranty issue. Get this sorted by noon, or I am calling Titan Motors to replace the entire fleet."

He hangs up before you can answer. The room is still dark. Outside, the corridor is already awake: reefers humming, drivers checking manifests, dispatch boards filling with loads that will not wait for a soot filter to clear itself.

Two years ago your sales team sold Falcon fifty new prime movers with 11-liter engines. A hard win over Titan Motors after months of TCO decks and overnight test runs. Sales sells the first truck. Service sells every truck after that. You have repeated that line in training rooms. This morning it does not feel like a slogan. It feels like a deadline.

You open the corporate warranty portal on your laptop before you put on coffee. Four of his 11L units threw critical engine codes overnight. Diagnosis: catastrophic Diesel Particulate Filter and Exhaust Gas Recirculation valve clogs. The engines choked on their own soot. The system already flagged the claims: **Code 404: Environmental/Operational Damage. WARRANTY REJECTED.**

You grab your keys. You need to know what killed those trucks before you walk into the yard, and you need to get there before Vikram's noon clock runs out.

---

**Summit Commercial Vehicles**

Summit is your brand's exclusive dealer for the corridor. By 7:40 AM the service bays are loud: pneumatic tools, diesel, degreaser, a radio someone forgot to turn down. Four Falcon units sit in the overflow lot with limp-mode tags on the windscreens. Cargo tarps still tied. Nobody has started wrenching.

Krit, the Dealer Service Head, measures success by Service Absorption. Parts and labour pay the dealership's operating cost. He protects that margin the way other people protect a house. He walks you to a workbench. A black, soot-choked EGR valve sits there like a paperweight.

"Look at this," Krit says. "This is not a manufacturing defect. This is driver abuse. Falcon's drivers idle twelve hours a day for the air conditioning, and they buy cheap diesel from unverified highland stops. The soot choked the system."

You remind him Falcon bought the Premium Predictive Maintenance contract. Vikram expects this handled as a partnership problem, not a parking problem.

"Corporate rejected the warranty," Krit says. "I am not fixing four trucks for free. My mechanics do not work for free. Peak season. Every bay hour has a queue behind it."

You tell him Falcon is the biggest account in the territory. If they leave for Titan, Summit loses the maintenance contract on all fifty trucks, not only these four.

Krit crosses his arms. "Then you figure it out. Until someone signs my invoice, those four trucks stay parked in my lot."

A mechanic glances over from bay three and looks away. Everyone in the building already knows whose name is on the phone log.

---

**Elena and the Goodwill line**

You step into the dealer's glass conference room and call Elena Rostova, National Service Director. She answers on the second ring. She has already seen the Code 404 flags.

You lay it out: Vikram is threatening to leave. The warranty rejection looks legitimate on fuel quality and idle behaviour. Krit wants retail. Noon is still on Vikram's clock.

"We cannot authorize a standard warranty claim if the data shows operational abuse," Elena says. "If an auditor sees we pushed that through, it comes out of my paycheck. You have your quarterly Goodwill budget. You can absorb some of the pain. Blow the whole budget on Vikram today and you have nothing left for the rest of the territory for three months."

You tell her Krit will not move on price, and that Titan is in Vikram's language already.

"Krit is trying to make a retail profit off a crisis," she says. "Squeeze him. He is the dealer. He needs to share the pain. Do not pay his retail invoice. Find a compromise, keep the trucks moving, and do not let Titan win. If you need Technical Bypass software or a DPF regen protocol on the table later, fine. First get the wheels turning."

She hangs up. It is mid-morning. Vikram's noon deadline is still live. Krit's invoice is still face-down on the printer tray. You have not opened the telematics yet, and you can already feel how little room there is between Elena's line and Vikram's temper.

---

**What the logs show**

You pull telematics, GPS, and service history for the four grounded trucks before you talk to anyone else. The conference room glass is streaked with bay dust. Outside, one of the Falcon units ticks as the engine cools.

Fuel sensors for the last three fill-ups show sulfur at **50ppm**. Your engines require a maximum of **15ppm**. Vikram has been buying cheaper highland diesel to shave cost on the long climbs. That alone is enough for Code 404 to stick on paper.

Idle time sits at **35 percent**. Normal for this corridor is under **15 percent**. Krit said the drivers were sleeping with the AC on. GPS tells a different story. The idle clusters are not at truck stops. They are at **Checkpoint Delta**, a mountain weigh station where trucks queue for hours while inspectors work through a backlog that never seems to clear. Operational reality, not laziness in a bunk. Whether Vikram will admit that without being shown the map is another question.

Then the maintenance log. Two weeks ago those trucks were in Krit's shop for their **40,000 km** preventative maintenance. Your corporate manual requires a **Manual DPF Burn-off** at that interval. The system log shows the step was skipped. Forty-five minutes of bay time saved on a busy day. If the burn-off had been done, the trucks might not have choked on the incline with freight still on the hook.

You sit with that for a minute. Fuel quality cuts against Vikram. Checkpoint Delta complicates the idle story. The skipped burn-off cuts against Krit. None of this is a speech you deliver in order. It is leverage you carry, and questions you ask, depending on what Vikram will actually hear when he walks in.

---

**The invoice and the bleed**

Krit finally slides the retail invoice across the table. Four trucks. **$14,000** total: **$2,500** parts and **$1,000** labour per truck (ten hours at **$100** per hour). That is what he wants Vikram, or you, to pay before a wrench turns.

What Krit actually pays is lower. Dealer net parts sit around **$1,500** per truck. Mechanic wage cost is closer to **$400** per truck. His floor for four trucks is roughly **$7,600**. He will not volunteer that number, and Elena was clear you should not pay as if the floor did not exist.

Your quarterly Goodwill budget is **$8,000**. Retail for four trucks already exceeds it. You cannot write a blank cheque, clear Vikram's rage, and still have cover for the next breakdown in the territory.

Falcon's bleed is simpler and louder. **$600** per day per grounded truck. Four trucks. **$2,400** a day. They have already been down two days. Penalties on perishable cargo do not care about sulfur ppm debates. Vikram will walk in with that number first.

Titan Motors has been circling Falcon for months with a lower sticker and a simpler emissions story. Vikram has a red folder with their logo on it. Whether the offer inside is real or a prop for this meeting, he will put it on the table. You have seen that folder once before, at a quarterly review, closed. Today it will open.

Prevention options exist on your side of the house if the conversation gets that far: DPF regen discipline, Technical Bypass software where approved, tighter PM checklists so a burn-off cannot be skipped quietly again. Elena named them. She also said they are useless if the trucks are still sitting in the lot while you argue about who loved the partnership more.

---

**Vikram before the storm**

Vikram Singh has run technical operations at Falcon for more than six years. Fifty trucks on the national corridor. Uptime, cost per kilometre, and a board that reads downtime line items every quarter. Drivers call him early. Dispatch calls him earlier. He answers both.

He is not a cartoon. He likes your trucks when they work. He has dealt fairly with your people in better years, including a winter when Summit pulled two units back onto the road overnight and he sent Krit a short thank-you that surprised everyone. He also remembers **2019**, when eight Falcon units failed within eighteen months and ended up in a corner of the yard the drivers still call the Graveyard. Warranty fights. Dealer runarounds. That residue is in every conversation with him now, whether he opens with it or not.

Your predecessor on this corridor lasted eighteen months and left a CRM note that said "Vikram prefers data over reassurance" and nothing about highland fuel or Checkpoint Delta queues. You learned the rest the hard way, truck by truck, invoice by invoice.

Yesterday, before the four units died, Vikram sat alone in his office after a corridor board call and stared at a utilisation chart with empty slots where those trucks should have been earning. A coffee gone cold. A Titan one-pager in the same red folder, still unread. He did not call Titan then. He called you at six this morning. That sequence matters. He is furious. He is also still choosing to be in the room.

---

**1:00 PM in the conference room**

Noon passes. The trucks are still in the lot. Your phone has three missed calls from Vikram and one short text from Elena: **Do not pay retail. Get them moving.**

At 1:00 PM Vikram storms into the conference room. He looks exhausted and furious. Krit walks in behind him with the **$14,000** invoice and does not bother with a handshake.

Vikram throws a bright red Titan Motors folder onto the table. Krit crosses his arms and does not sit. The invoice stays face-up between you and the empty chair across from Vikram. Somewhere in the bay, a pneumatic gun fires and stops. The glass walls make the silence feel louder than it is.

Vikram stands, hands flat on the table, eyes on you. He is about to speak.

Your laptop is open. You have the fuel numbers, the Checkpoint Delta idle pattern, the skipped burn-off, the retail invoice, Krit's real floor, prevention options Elena named, and an **$8,000** Goodwill budget that will not stretch forever. How you use any of it depends on what he says first, and what you say back.
