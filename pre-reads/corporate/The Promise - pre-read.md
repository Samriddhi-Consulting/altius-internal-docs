# The Promise

You are not the only one who wants that shelf.

Your area salesman, Deepak, heard it over chai from Rajesh Khurana's godown manager: three new snack brands have pitched Khurana Distributors this month. A protein chips brand out of Mumbai is offering 12 percent distributor margin. A masala makhana brand from Jaipur is offering 12 plus a launch display scheme. Then there's yours, at the category-standard 8.

On Khurana's whiteboard, apparently, the three names sit in a column, in the marker he still buys by the box because he doesn't trust the tablet his son installed last year. Yours has a question mark next to it. Nobody drew a line through it. Nobody drew a tick either.

Tomorrow at 11 AM you get forty minutes with the man himself, and it is not your first attempt. Three months ago, in your first week on this territory, you sent Khurana the standard national deck by courier and followed it with one phone call that didn't get returned. You told yourself he was busy. Looking back, you know exactly what a courier and one call look like to a man who has been pitched by six hundred people: forgettable.

You are the firm's Key Account Manager for Delhi NCR, the first and only one, and there is no route into West Delhi's general trade that does not pass through his godown. Roughly 1,400 outlets stock what he tells them will move, because he has been right for twenty-eight years. The city launch plan you presented to the board has his network written into the first-quarter numbers. Nobody asked whether he had agreed. That is your job, and this time you are not sending a deck.

---

**The Company You Carry**

Your firm is three and a half years old. Roasted makhana in four flavours, made by a co-packer in Sonipat, sold only online until now.

Eight months ago it closed a Series A of Rs 40 crore from a well-known consumer fund. The press release said "category-defining." The board's brief for the money is one word: offline. Online works, about 70 percent of revenue through quick commerce and marketplaces, 3x growth last year. But everyone on the cap table knows how Indian snacking really works: the brands that last are the brands on shelves. You have four quarters to prove the firm can sell where the internet ends, and Delhi NCR is quarter one.

You are not the only KAM carrying that mandate. Bangalore closed two distributors last month without giving up a single margin point, and Kabir, who runs operations and signs off your terms, mentioned it twice in the same board update, both times without your name attached, which somehow made it land harder.

Part of that Rs 40 crore went somewhere unglamorous. In March, the Sonipat co-packer's line capacity was doubled, and the firm has committed in writing to its quick-commerce partners that it will hold 45 days of finished festive inventory from mid-September onward. Diwali is twelve weeks away.

---

**What Shoppers Already Know**

The consumer story is the part you could recite in your sleep. A 41 percent repeat rate on quick commerce, high for snacking. Peri Peri Makhana selling out most weekends in South Delhi. A 4.4 rating across platforms. A nutritionist with two million followers calling it her airport snack, unpaid.

And one number quieter than the rest, from the twelve pilot stores the firm ran in Gurugram modern trade last quarter: the product rotated fully every 11 days. The snack category norm in those same stores is 18 to 21 days. Put it in front of a shopper and it moves.

What you don't have, and what Khurana will likely ask for, is a single day of data from a shop like his. Twelve air-conditioned modern trade stores in Gurugram are not 1,400 general trade counters in West Delhi, and a distributor who has run this business for twenty-eight years will know the difference before you finish the sentence.

If the meeting turns to money, and it will, the numbers you carry are these: your launch plan projects Rs 8 lakh a quarter through his network in year one, at whatever margin you agree. The gap on the table is four points. What that gap is worth to him, per quarter and per year, and what an 11-day rotation is worth against an 18-day one, is arithmetic you should be able to do on the back of his own ledger paper. He will check it. He always checks it. Khurana thinks in turns, not case studies.

---

**The Man Behind the Godown**

Rajesh Khurana took over his father's two tempos and a ledger in 1997. His shelf, he likes to say, is a promise he makes to 1,400 shopkeepers. He carries two national snack brands and four regional ones, and drops maybe one brand a year, reluctantly. He still does his accounts by hand on Sunday mornings, ledger open on the same desk his father used, and checks the software against it, not the other way round.

Deepak has been on this territory four years and has watched Khurana operate the way younger salesmen watch cricket. "He went to Tilak Nagar himself last monsoon," Deepak told you once, unprompted. "One retailer, one damaged carton dispute, maybe two thousand rupees. The company was stalling. Khurana Sahib paid it from his own pocket, then fought the company for six weeks to get it back. The retailer tells everyone that story. That's why nobody in Tilak Nagar stocks anything Khurana hasn't touched."

Six hundred people have pitched him. He has heard every version of the deck you almost sent again.

---

**What Deepak Heard**

From Deepak, and from a Vikaspuri retailer who talks more than he should, you've picked up fragments about last Diwali.

A funded brand, loud marketing, a name Khurana pushed hard into his own network on his own word. Something went wrong in the festive weeks. The Vikaspuri retailer wouldn't say how badly. What he did say stuck with you.

"Khurana Sahib came here himself, twice, in Diwali week," the retailer said, weighing out sugar while he talked. "Not his salesman. Him. First time to say the stock was coming. Second time to say it wasn't. He stood where you're standing and apologised for a company that wasn't his. Twenty years I've bought from him. I've never seen him do that for anyone."

You asked Deepak what happened to the brand. He shrugged. "Gone from the whiteboard. Gone from the godown. He doesn't say the name. The godown boys don't either, not in front of him."

It isn't the kind of story a man like Khurana opens a meeting with, and nobody has offered to fill in the rest.

His message to you, relayed through Deepak, was one line: "Everyone new is offering 12. You're offering 8. My shelf is full. Bring a better number or don't come."

---

**The Other Two Names**

You've done what homework you can on the competition.

The protein chips brand out of Mumbai is eighteen months old, gym-adjacent positioning, strong on social media. Deepak checked two modern trade stores in Janakpuri that stock it: the packets on the shelf carried a manufacturing date from eleven weeks ago. Nobody restocks what isn't moving.

The masala makhana brand from Jaipur is closer to home, same category as you, and their launch display scheme is real: a branded rack, free, for any outlet that takes the first order. Racks look good in a pitch. Deepak's read, for what it's worth: "Racks fill a corner. They don't empty it."

Neither brand has run a Delhi festive season. Neither has published a fill rate. Both are offering 12.

---

**Your Mandate**

Your terms, signed off by Kabir before you left the office: margin at 8 percent, no authority to go higher. Claims and damages settled in 7 days, against an industry habit of 30 to 45. Fill rate over the last two quarters, 96.4 percent, monthly sheet in your bag. For a first order you may offer sale-or-return on non-moving stock and guaranteed 48-hour replacement on damages.

You raised the obvious question before leaving. "If he holds at 12?"

"Then you find out why 12 matters to him," Kabir said, not looking up from his screen. "Do not buy the shelf. If we pay 12 in West Delhi, we pay 12 everywhere, forever. Find out what he actually needs."

There is a cautionary tale inside your own supply chain. The firm's co-packer has a sales head, new to trade two years ago, who held a line so hard on a Lucknow distributor that he never once explained the arithmetic behind it. He said "policy" until the man stopped taking his calls, and lost a route the firm still doesn't have back. Holding a number and holding a room are not the same skill. You are about to find out whether you have the second one.

---

**11 AM**

The office above the godown smells of cardboard and old fan grease, and somewhere below a weighing scale keeps banging shut between customers. On the wall behind the desk, the whiteboard. Three names in a column. Yours still has the question mark.

Rajesh Khurana reads your one-pager with the face of a man who has read six hundred one-pagers, then sets it down, turns it face-down on the desk the way a man closes a matter, and looks at you.



The room holds. The clock holds. The next honest sentence is the only lever left in this scene, and it has to be earned rather than performed. You have the facts. You have the pressure. What you do not have is a script that survives contact with the person across from you if you lead with the easy story instead of the true one. Stay with the specifics. Leave room. Do not rescue the conversation with a brochure answer. The meeting starts when they decide you are listening.
---

**Before You Sit Down**

The godown smell hits first: cardboard, spice dust, diesel from the lane. Rajesh's whiteboard is not decoration. It is a ledger of who apologised and who did not. Forty minutes is not long when the alternative brands already offered twelve. Your job is not to buy the shelf with margin. It is to make eight percent feel like the safer promise than twelve percent from strangers who have never run a Delhi festive season. The clock above the godown door ticks once, then disappears into street noise. He will not fill silence for you.

The three brands on the whiteboard are not abstract competitors. They are names Rajesh has already tested against the same shopkeeper promises you are about to ask him to risk again. He will not say Snackly first. He will say twelve percent. Your discovery has to earn the story under the margin. Forty minutes. Eight percent. One promise that has to survive a festive season.
