# The Unnamed Source

Ananya Iyer sent the first message on a Tuesday evening, two weeks before any of this had a file number.

You were three months into your HR Business Partner rotation at Kestrel Analytics, a mid-size data and consulting firm, and most of those weeks had been onboarding paperwork, policy explainers, and sitting in the back of meetings while someone senior did the talking. As part of the rotation you had been assigned as an informal onboarding buddy to a handful of new hires from the last intake. Ananya was one of them, a junior analyst on Client Delivery. Your job was the small stuff: where to find the leave form, which Slack channel actually answered, how to survive the first client deck review without treating every red pen mark as a verdict on your career.

Her message that Tuesday was not small.

She had come out of a team meeting upset. An idea she raised had been brushed aside mid-sentence. Ten minutes later a teammate repackaged the same idea and Rohan Vashisht, her manager, praised it. You told her that sounded frustrating and left it there. Buddy conversation. Nothing more.

She replied twenty minutes later with a screenshot of her own slide still open on her laptop, the one she had stopped mid-sentence. The repackaged version was in the shared deck with someone else's name on the footer. She had not asked you to fix it. She had asked whether this was normal here. You did not have a good answer.

It happened again the following week. Then she started describing a pattern going back months: being talked over in meetings, watching the same two or three people get the interesting client work while she and others got maintenance tasks. You encouraged her to put it in writing. Not because you wanted this outcome, but because "this has been happening for months and no one knows" felt like the wrong place to leave it. She was hesitant, worried about what filing would mean for her standing on the team. Last week she submitted a formal complaint naming Rohan, with an explicit request that her identity be withheld from him at this stage.

You know exactly who filed. Priya Nanda, your reporting manager and the firm's HR Director, knows too. That part is on file. What Priya does not know, because you never mentioned it, is that Ananya came to you first, that you two talk fairly often, and that if Rohan starts working through the roster in his head, you are one of the people whose name might surface before hers does.

---

**Three Months In**

Kestrel Analytics sells analytics and consulting to clients who want numbers they can act on and teams that can deliver under deadline. Client Delivery is one of the sharper-performing groups in the firm. Rohan Vashisht runs it, twelve people, six years at Kestrel, promoted into management two years ago. Leadership likes him. His team's numbers are good. He has never had a complaint filed against him before.

Until last week.

Your rotation was supposed to be observational at first: sit with employee relations cases, shadow exit interviews, learn where the handbook lives. Priya Nanda runs a tight shop. She does not hand sensitive conversations to new rotations casually. Today is different. Today you are the one who has to walk into a room.

An hour ago she called you into her office and did not ease into it.

"There's a formal complaint on file. About Rohan Vashisht."

---

**The Complaint**

One complaint. That is what exists on record. It describes a pattern, not a single incident: being talked over in meetings, ideas dismissed and then credited elsewhere, visible favoritism in how client work gets assigned. Ananya asked that her name not go to Rohan at this stage. No corroborating complaints. Rohan's team scored average on the last engagement survey, unremarkable, nothing that would have flagged this on its own.

Priya was clear about scope before you left her office.

"Your job today is not to build a case. It's not an investigation. We're nowhere near that stage, and it might never get there. Your job is a first conversation: surface the pattern, gauge how he responds, and get a genuine next step out of it if you can. Her name does not leave this building. Not to Rohan, not today, not if he guesses correctly and asks you to confirm it. That's not optional."

You asked what happens if he pushes on that.

"He will push on that. Rohan is smart, and he's going to ask the question you'd ask if you were in his position: how am I supposed to defend myself against something I can't even see clearly? That's a fair question. You don't have a clean answer to it. What you have is a process, and your job is to hold that process without either hiding behind it like a shield or apologizing for it like it's unreasonable."

She paused.

"One more thing. Don't walk in there ready to convict him. We know who filed this, but we don't know yet if it's a serious problem, a blind spot, or someone having a hard few months and reaching a breaking point. Your job is to find out, not to have already decided."

You are not deciding discipline today. You are not issuing a warning, placing him on a plan, or telling him what HR will do next beyond what this conversation earns. Priya will handle anything formal. Your authority is real but bounded: first fact-surfacing conversation, hold the confidentiality line absolutely, aim for a next step if you can.

---

**Who You Are Walking Toward**

Rohan is thirty-four, sharp, well-liked by leadership, and by most accounts well-liked by his team. He thinks of himself, genuinely, as a fair manager. Maybe a demanding one, but fair.

He is quick and direct, used to being the most capable person in most rooms he walks into. He does not reach for corporate language, does not hedge, and asks pointed questions rather than making speeches. If he feels cornered, expect dry, cutting humor before you see any raised voice. The room will stay quiet. The pressure will feel more like a cross-examination, calm on the surface, sharp underneath.

He has no idea this meeting is coming. Priya's assistant booked thirty minutes on his calendar this morning labeled simply "HR check-in." He does not know what "check-in" means yet. He is about to find out.

You have thought about him twice in the last month without connecting either thought to today. Once when Ananya mentioned a Monday stand-up where he cut someone off to chase a client deadline and the room treated it as normal. Once when Priya cited Client Delivery's utilization numbers in a staff meeting and Rohan got a nod from the COO that looked like recognition, not scrutiny. Both memories feel different now that you are carrying a folder you are not going to open on the table.

A colleague in the rotation cohort told you last month about her first confidential conversation: she leaked without meaning to, confirming a guess with a half-second pause and a change of subject. Her manager rebuilt the case from scratch with a different interviewer. The story stayed with you because the failure mode was not malice. It was composure.

---

**What You Carry In**

The complaint is a signal, not a verdict. One person filed. That does not make the pattern false. It also does not make Rohan guilty before he has had a chance to respond.

You also carry something Priya does not fully see: you encouraged Ananya to write it down. You were her buddy before you were HR on this case. That closeness is invisible to Rohan and it is a liability if it surfaces even indirectly. A defensive tone when he gets close to guessing. An instinct to over-correct away from a name he mentions. A pause that lasts half a second too long. Confidentiality lives in composure when the right name is in the air, not only in the words you refuse to say.

There is a narrow path here, and it will not feel comfortable. You are protecting a specific person whose name you know, in a conversation with the one manager most motivated to work out who she is, about behaviour you can only describe in the abstract: being talked over, credit moving in meetings, who gets which client assignments. Rohan will feel that abstraction and press on it. Holding the line and actually talking about the behaviour are both the job, at the same time, which is most of what makes this hard.

Rohan may be genuinely rattled by facing an unnamed accusation. That feeling is legitimate. It is separate from whether the underlying pattern is true. Your job is to hear both threads without trading the boundary for relief, and without treating his frustration about the process as proof the complaint is false.

You cannot fix this in thirty minutes. A believable next step matters more than a forced apology or a denial that goes nowhere: coaching, a changed meeting habit, a follow-up check-in in a few weeks. Something concrete enough that Priya can work with, vague enough that you are not deciding his fate in this room.

The clock is real. Priya expects a read before end of day. Rohan has client deadlines this afternoon and will not grant you unlimited time to find the right tone. If the conversation spends the full thirty minutes on whether he is allowed to know who accused him, you will leave having tested nothing about the pattern itself.

---

**Entering the Room**

The small meeting room on the fourth floor is glass-walled and already occupied when you arrive. Rohan is checking his phone, mildly curious rather than concerned. A half-finished coffee sits near the edge of the table. Through the glass you can see Client Delivery's open plan beyond, keyboards and stand-up boards, the ordinary rhythm of a team that does not know what is happening behind this door.

He looks up and gestures at the chair across from him.

"HR check-in, huh? That's a first for me." He sets the phone down. "What's going on?"

You sit. Priya's instructions are in your head. A single anonymous complaint sits in a folder you are not going to open on the table. Ananya's name sits in your head. Rohan is waiting for an answer that will tell him whether this is routine or something else.

Between now and the end of these thirty minutes, he will either engage with a pattern he may not fully see in himself, or spend the time fighting you on whether the conversation is fair without a name attached. What he will not get from you today is who filed. What you still have to earn is whether anything real happens after that line holds.
